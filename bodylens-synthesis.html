The full picture — what every system taught us
The body runs on a single currency: energy. Everything else is how that energy is produced, allocated, and protected. ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the only form of energy the body can actually use — every system ultimately serves its production and efficient use.
1
Mitochondria are the ATP factories — Zone 2 builds them
Mitochondrial density in muscle and organ cells determines how much sustainable energy is available — not just for exercise, but for cognition, immunity, and recovery. Zone 2 training is the primary stimulus for mitochondrial biogenesis. Glycaemic stability is the day-to-day energy management variable — the insulin spike from a high-GI meal produces a blood glucose crash 90-120 minutes later, impacting every system that follows. Mitochondria = energy infrastructure · Zone 2 builds it · flat glucose maintains it.
2
The gut is not a digestion tube — it is the primary mood regulator
The enteric nervous system — 500 million neurons lining the gut — communicates directly with the brain via the vagus nerve. 95% of serotonin is produced in the gut. Gut health is mood health — dysbiosis (microbial imbalance) mechanistically produces anxiety, low mood, and cognitive impairment through inflammatory signalling, not as a metaphor. The Bristol Stool Chart is the daily gut health diagnostic: Type 4 consistently = fibre, hydration, and motility working. Anything else tells you what failed yesterday.
3
Hormones are the operating system — they determine what the body does with every input
Testosterone, cortisol, GH, insulin, leptin, ghrelin, oestrogen — these are not independent variables. They form an interconnected system where each affects the others. Testosterone and cortisol are inversely related — chronic stress suppresses testosterone not just through the HPT axis but through direct competition. GH is released primarily during slow-wave sleep — which alcohol suppresses. Insulin sensitivity determines whether calories go to muscle or fat. Every lifestyle choice either improves or degrades the hormonal environment.
4
Inflammation is the root cause under every chronic disease and performance limitation
Low-grade systemic inflammation — from poor gut health, sleep deprivation, excessive body fat, processed food, chronic stress, and alcohol — impairs every system simultaneously. It degrades insulin sensitivity, suppresses testosterone, elevates cortisol, impairs recovery, reduces cognitive function, and accelerates ageing at the cellular level. The anti-inflammatory protocol — omega-3, olive oil, polyphenols, adequate sleep, resistance training — addresses the root, not the symptoms.

Feedback loops — where systems amplify each other
The most important insight in systems biology: variables do not simply add together — they amplify each other through feedback loops. Understanding which loops you are in determines whether you are in an upward or downward spiral.
Sleep → Testosterone → Training quality → Sleep
Virtuous
Adequate sleep (8h) → GH pulses in slow-wave sleep, testosterone synthesised overnight → training quality high the next day, progressive overload achieved → training stimulus drives further GH release → improved sleep architecture. The upward loop: protect the sleep, the rest follows.
Poor sleep → Cortisol → Visceral fat → Insulin resistance → Worse sleep
Vicious
Sub-6h sleep → cortisol elevated throughout next day → cortisol drives visceral fat accumulation and impairs insulin sensitivity → insulin resistance elevates blood glucose at night → impairs sleep architecture → worse sleep the following night. Breaking the loop: sleep quality improvement produces metabolic improvement that further improves sleep.
Protein → MPS → Muscle → Metabolic rate → Fat loss → Better body composition
Virtuous
Adequate protein (your target) → mTOR activation → MPS elevated → lean mass maintained or built → higher resting metabolic rate → improved insulin sensitivity → better fat oxidation → improved body composition → easier to maintain protein intake on correct calories. Each link strengthens the next.
Gut health → Serotonin → Mood → Adherence → Gut health
Virtuous
Diverse fibre intake → healthy microbiome → serotonin production supported → better baseline mood → higher motivation and adherence to programme → consistent training and nutrition → reinforces gut health through omega-3, anti-inflammatory eating, and reduced stress. The gut-mood-adherence loop is the most underappreciated determinant of long-term success.

How nutrition runs the machine
Every training adaptation, hormonal function, cognitive process, and recovery mechanism runs on substrate provided by nutrition. Food is not fuel in the car metaphor — it is the building material, the signalling molecule, and the cofactor for every process simultaneously.
1
Protein is the structural and signalling molecule — not just building material
Amino acids are the structural unit of muscle, enzymes, hormones, neurotransmitters, and immune proteins simultaneously. The leucine threshold (2.5g leucine per meal) triggers mTOR — the molecular switch for muscle protein synthesis. Missing the threshold in any meal means no MPS regardless of total daily protein. This is why distribution matters as much as total: your daily protein target distributed across 3-4 meals, each clearing 25g+ complete protein, outperforms the same total consumed in one or two meals.
2
Carbohydrates are the performance fuel — timing determines their function
Muscle glycogen is the primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity exercise. Training without adequate glycogen reduces performance, impairs the training stimulus, and elevates cortisol (glycogen depletion is a stress signal). Post-training carbohydrates replenish glycogen and — combined with protein — produce an insulin spike that drives nutrient partitioning toward muscle. The training-day carbohydrate allocation is not optional — it is the performance and recovery substrate. Rest-day carbohydrates are reduced because glycogen demand is lower.
3
Dietary fat is the hormonal precursor — the minimum matters as much as the maximum
Testosterone, oestrogen, cortisol, and all steroid hormones are synthesised from cholesterol — which requires dietary fat. Reducing fat below approximately 20% of calories impairs testosterone production and hormonal signalling across the board. Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) are the anti-inflammatory substrate — competing with arachidonic acid for COX enzymes, directly reducing systemic inflammation. Fat is not the enemy of body composition — chronically insufficient fat impairs the hormonal environment that makes training adaptation possible.

The recovery cycle — where adaptation actually happens
Training is the stimulus. Recovery is the adaptation. Most people understand this conceptually — fewer understand that recovery is an active, complex biological process with specific requirements, not simply the absence of training.
1
The signal-to-adaptation timeline
Training creates: muscle damage (micro-tears in myofibrils), metabolic stress (depletion of ATP, PCr, glycogen), and mechanical tension (stretching of sarcomeres under load). Each triggers a cascade. Inflammatory signalling recruits satellite cells. mTOR activates protein synthesis. GH and IGF-1 are released. The adaptation — actual muscle growth, strength increase, neural efficiency — occurs in the 24-72 hours after training, not during it. Sleep, protein, and adequate calories during this window determine whether the stimulus produces adaptation or simply damage.
2
Sleep is where the adaptation is claimed
GH pulses during slow-wave sleep — the primary anabolic hormone driving tissue repair. Testosterone is synthesised. The glymphatic system (the brain's waste clearance network, 60% more active during sleep) removes metabolic byproducts from the CNS. Memory consolidation occurs — both procedural memory (motor patterns) and declarative memory. Training sends the signal. Sleep claims the adaptation. Missing sleep after training means the stimulus happened but much of the adaptation did not.
3
Active recovery tools — what your stack does
Sauna (80-100°C, 20 min): heat shock protein induction, plasma volume expansion (mimicking cardiac training), GH release. Cold plunge (10-15°C, 3-5 min): vasoconstriction → vasodilation, catecholamine surge, inflammation modulation. Swimming/pool: unloads joints while maintaining blood flow and light cardiovascular stimulus. Each tool has a mechanism — none replace sleep and protein, but all support the recovery environment in specific ways.
