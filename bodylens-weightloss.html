The fundamental equation — true and incomplete
CICO — calories in, calories out — is the most argued concept in nutrition. It is simultaneously the most correct and the most misleading framework in existence. Here is why both are true.
1
Energy balance is thermodynamically inviolable — fat storage requires a caloric surplus
The first law of thermodynamics applies to human metabolism without exception. You cannot store fat without consuming more energy than you expend. No diet, food combination, hormone protocol, or timing strategy circumvents the energy balance equation. The debate is not whether energy balance governs fat gain and loss. It does. The debate is about what determines the variables on each side — and that is where it gets interesting.
2
Why CICO is massively incomplete — "calories out" is not a fixed number
The critical flaw in simple CICO thinking: treating total daily energy expenditure as a fixed, calculable number that does not respond to what you eat, how you exercise, or how much you've previously dieted. In reality, TDEE is a dynamic, adaptive variable — it responds to deficit, to training, to stress, to sleep, to hormonal status. Your body defends its fat stores with considerable biological sophistication.
3
Where fat actually goes when you lose it — you breathe it out
This is one of the most misunderstood facts in biology. Fat (triglyceride) is broken down through lipolysis into fatty acids and glycerol. Through beta-oxidation, these become acetyl-CoA which enters the Krebs cycle, producing CO₂ and H₂O. Approximately 84% of the weight lost during fat loss leaves the body through the lungs as carbon dioxide. The remainder leaves as water through sweat, urine, and breath. Fat does not "convert" to muscle. It does not leave through sweat or waste.

TDEE has four components — most people only think about one
Total Daily Energy Expenditure is not just "exercise." It has four distinct components that behave very differently — and only one of them is directly under your control in the way most people think.
BMR
60–70%
Basal Metabolic Rate
Energy to stay alive at rest. Determined by lean mass, age, sex, and genetics. The dominant component — and the one that adapts when you diet.
TEF
8–15%
Thermic Effect of Food
Energy to digest and process food. Protein has the highest TEF (20-35% of its calories). Fat has the lowest (2-3%). Eating more protein slightly increases TDEE.
EAT
5–15%
Exercise Activity
Formal training. The component most people focus on — but actually a smaller fraction of total expenditure than assumed. Highly variable and adaptive.
NEAT
15–50%
Non-Exercise Activity
Everything else — walking, fidgeting, posture, spontaneous movement. Enormously variable. This is the component that changes most dramatically during dieting — and explains most of diet failure.
!
NEAT is the component that makes or breaks fat loss — and is almost entirely unconscious
When you go into a caloric deficit, your body reduces NEAT automatically — you move less, fidget less, sit more, take shorter steps. In severe caloric restriction, NEAT can drop by 400-600 kcal per day. This is not willpower. It is a coordinated biological response to energy shortage — driven by leptin, thyroid hormones, and the hypothalamus. This is why adding more exercise to a severe diet often produces less fat loss than expected: the body compensates by reducing NEAT.

The four hormones that govern fat loss
Hormones don't override energy balance. But they profoundly affect how easy or hard energy balance is to maintain — by controlling hunger, satiety, energy expenditure, and fat cell behaviour.
1
Leptin — the satiety and metabolic rate signal from fat cells
Leptin is produced by fat cells in proportion to how much fat you carry. It signals the hypothalamus that you have adequate energy stores, suppressing hunger and maintaining metabolic rate. During fat loss, leptin drops — which is why hunger increases and metabolic rate slows as you get leaner. This is an evolutionary feature: your body interprets fat loss as famine and fights back. Leptin sensitivity (how well the brain responds to leptin) is impaired by obesity, sleep deprivation, and chronic stress.
2
Ghrelin — the hunger hormone that rises during deficit
Ghrelin is the primary hunger-signalling hormone — it rises before meals and drops after eating. During caloric restriction, ghrelin baseline levels rise — meaning you are genuinely physiologically hungrier in a deficit, not just lacking willpower. This elevation persists for months after weight loss, which explains why maintaining weight loss is harder than losing it. The body continues signalling hunger long after the deficit has ended.
3
Insulin — the fat storage gatekeeper
Insulin's role in fat loss is frequently misrepresented. The truth: you cannot store fat without insulin present, but insulin is not the primary driver of fat gain — caloric surplus is. High insulin (from high carbohydrate intake) does inhibit lipolysis (fat burning) — but only in the context of adequate energy intake. In a caloric deficit, even high insulin cannot prevent fat loss. The carbohydrate-insulin model of obesity overstates insulin's role. Insulin sensitivity, however, dramatically affects how easily you access fat stores and how much muscle you preserve during a deficit.
4
Cortisol — the stress hormone that drives fat storage and muscle loss
Cortisol mobilises energy in response to stress — useful acutely, deeply counterproductive chronically. Chronically elevated cortisol: breaks down muscle tissue (gluconeogenesis — converting protein to glucose), drives visceral fat accumulation specifically (cortisol receptors are dense in abdominal fat cells), elevates insulin, and directly impairs leptin signalling. You cannot out-diet chronic stress. Sleep deprivation, excessive training volume, and psychological stress all chronically elevate cortisol and make fat loss harder at the hormonal level.

Why diets fail — the four adaptive responses
The failure rate of caloric restriction diets is not a character flaw problem. It is a biology problem. The body has four distinct adaptive mechanisms designed to restore lost fat. Understanding them is the prerequisite for not being defeated by them.
1
Metabolic adaptation — the BMR drops more than lean mass loss explains
When you go into a caloric deficit, BMR falls. Some of this is expected — less body mass means less energy to maintain it. But research consistently shows that BMR falls more than lean mass loss alone explains. The body actively downregulates thyroid hormone (T3), reduces sympathetic nervous system activity, and decreases the cellular energy cost of basic processes. This "adaptive thermogenesis" can amount to 100-300 kcal per day of extra reduction — a meaningful headwind for fat loss maintenance.
2
NEAT collapse — unconscious movement drops dramatically in deficit
As described in TDEE Components — when you diet, your body automatically reduces non-exercise movement. Fidgeting stops. You take fewer steps. You sit more. You unconsciously reduce all the small movements that burn 200-500 kcal per day. This is not a decision. It is a hypothalamic response coordinated by leptin, NPY, and orexin systems. Adding more gym sessions does not compensate — the body just reduces NEAT further to preserve energy balance.
3
Leptin drops and hunger escalates — the hormonal fight-back
As fat mass decreases, leptin production decreases. Lower leptin signals the hypothalamus to increase hunger, reduce satiety, and trigger metabolic slowdown. This is the system working exactly as designed — protecting against starvation. The result: the leaner you get, the hungrier you become and the slower your metabolism runs. This is why the last 5-10% of fat loss is dramatically harder than the first 20% — you are fighting an increasingly organised biological resistance.
4
Fat cells never disappear — they wait to refill
Adipocytes (fat cells) shrink during fat loss but they do not die. The number of fat cells you have is largely determined in childhood and adolescence and remains relatively fixed throughout adult life. Empty fat cells produce signals (resistin, RBP4) that drive hunger and reduce insulin sensitivity — essentially demanding to be refilled. This is a real biological pressure that persists after weight loss and contributes to regain. You can stay lean — but the pressure to refill is real and permanent.
Fat loss vs weight loss — they are not the same
The scale measures everything: fat, muscle, water, glycogen, food in transit, bone. Most "weight loss" is not fat loss. And much of what appears to be fat loss on the scale is not. The distinction matters enormously for programme design.
1
Glycogen and water — the first week is mostly this
Each gram of glycogen is stored with 3-4g of water. A standard carbohydrate restriction drops glycogen stores by 300-500g — taking 1-2kg of water with it. This is not fat loss. It returns immediately when carbohydrates are restored. This is why low-carb diets produce dramatic early weight loss — and why that weight returns within 48 hours of reintroducing carbohydrates. Understanding this prevents the demoralisation of "gaining back" weight that was never fat to begin with.
2
Muscle loss — the hidden cost of aggressive deficits
Without adequate protein and resistance training stimulus, caloric restriction produces significant muscle loss alongside fat loss. The body uses muscle protein as a gluconeogenic substrate during aggressive deficits — especially in the absence of training stimulus. At a 500 kcal/day deficit without strength training, up to 30-40% of weight lost may be lean mass. This is why scale weight is a poor proxy for body composition change and why the combination of resistance training + adequate protein is non-negotiable for fat loss that actually improves body composition.
3
The DEXA standard — why body composition measurement matters
DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) is the gold standard for body composition — it measures lean mass, fat mass, bone density, and visceral fat independently. The scale cannot tell you whether you've lost fat or muscle. Progress photos cannot either with precision. DEXA can. Attia recommends annual DEXA scans for anyone serious about long-term health and body composition tracking. The data is actionable in ways that scale weight is not.

What actually works — the evidence base
Given the adaptive responses above, the interventions that reliably produce sustained fat loss are those that work with — or around — the body's adaptive machinery rather than simply overriding it with willpower.
1
Moderate deficit + high protein + resistance training — the evidence-based combination
A 300-500 kcal deficit (not 1000+) reduces adaptive thermogenesis and NEAT collapse compared to aggressive restriction. High protein (1.8-2.4g/kg) maximises muscle preservation, increases satiety through TEF, and reduces the anabolic cost of dieting. Resistance training maintains the training stimulus for muscle protein synthesis, preventing the lean mass loss that occurs with diet alone. This combination outperforms any other approach in long-term body composition improvement.
2
Diet breaks and re-feeds — managing the hormonal adaptation
Periods at maintenance calories (1-2 weeks after every 4-8 weeks of deficit) partially restore leptin, reduce cortisol, and allow NEAT to recover. They do not meaningfully slow fat loss over a longer time horizon — the fat lost during the deficit is not regained in a maintenance week. They do meaningfully improve adherence, hormonal status, and training performance. Diet breaks are not cheating — they are a strategic tool for managing the biology.
3
Sleep — the underrated fat loss variable
Insufficient sleep (sub-6h) produces: elevated ghrelin (more hunger), reduced leptin (less satiety), elevated cortisol (more visceral fat storage and muscle breakdown), increased insulin resistance (poorer nutrient partitioning), and dramatically reduced NEAT. A single week of sleep restriction reduces fat loss by approximately 55% and increases lean mass loss — even when calories are identical. Sleep deprivation does not allow fat loss to continue at the expected rate regardless of dietary adherence.
