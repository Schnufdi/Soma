Section 01

Executive Summary

Soma occupies a genuine gap in a saturated market: it is not a fitness tracker, a calorie counter, or a motivational app. It is the first consumer product that treats its user as an intelligent adult and explains the biology behind every recommendation it makes. The science layer — why cortisol timing matters for caffeine, why mTOR windows are real, why pre-training meal composition is specific to the session — is better than most of what is freely available online. That is the product. The daily plan is the delivery mechanism.

Why now

LLMs crossed a threshold in 2023–24 that makes genuine personalisation at consumer scale economically viable for the first time. Previous attempts at intelligent health coaching required armies of human coaches (Future, Caliber) which made unit economics unworkable, or relied on wearable data without the explanatory layer (Whoop, Garmin). The combination of deep onboarding data, LLM reasoning, and a structured science architecture is only possible now.

Why these founders

The finance background is counterintuitive and exactly right. Eighteen years operating inside institutional complexity — managing information asymmetry, building analytical frameworks, communicating risk to non-experts — is precisely the skill set required to make complex biology legible to ordinary people. The marketing background addresses the hardest problem in consumer health: distribution in a crowded market. This combination is rare and genuinely well-suited to the problem.

Five-year success

50,000+ consumer subscribers, 5–8 white label partnerships with gym chains or health providers, 20+ corporate wellness contracts, £8–12M ARR, a recognisable brand in the "intelligent health" category, and a data asset that is beginning to attract research and commercial interest.

Honest probability

£2M ARR in 24 months — 60–65%. Achievable but requires executing the right things in the right order. £10M ARR in 48 months — 20–25%. Requires both the consumer product to prove the model and the B2B motion to work, which involves longer sales cycles and capital requirements the current budget cannot cover without either fundraising or organic profit from the consumer tier first.

The most likely failure mode is not a dramatic collapse. It is churn proving structurally difficult to reduce, the subscriber base plateauing at 3,000–4,000, and the business neither failing nor succeeding — just not compounding. The intervention that prevents this is retention engineering, not acquisition spending. That is where the focus must go first.

Market Analysis

The fitness app market is the wrong frame. The global fitness app market is projected at $15B by 2026 (Grand View Research, 2023). That number is real but misleading as a TAM for Soma because competing for a slice of MyFitnessPal's calorie-counting users is not the opportunity.

The real TAM

Personalised health and wellness — defined as products that use individual biological and behavioural data to generate genuinely specific guidance, not generic recommendations. This segment is smaller than "fitness apps" but has substantially higher willingness to pay and substantially lower competition. Estimated UK market for premium personalised health products: £400–600M annually, including private health coaching, nutritional consultancy, personal training, and premium digital health. UK digital penetration of this is currently under 5%, creating a clear runway.

SAM

UK adults who exercise 3+ times per week, are willing to pay £10–40/month for health guidance, and have sufficient digital literacy to engage with an AI-native product. This demographic is approximately 4.2M people (ONS 2022 Sport England Active Lives data: 28% of UK adults exercise at recommended levels; filtered for income bracket capable of subscription spend). At £39/month and 1% penetration of this segment, SAM revenue is £1.97B annually.

SOM — realistic 24-month

10,000–15,000 subscribers. At blended £13/month (mix of monthly and annual pricing), this is £1.56–2.34M ARR. This requires no extraordinary market conditions — it requires a working acquisition channel and retention above the category average of 7–8% monthly churn.

B2B market sizing

  • UK gym market: 7,200 gyms, 10.4M members (Leisure DB 2023). White label digital health at £3/member/month across 2% of members at 3 gym chains: £7.5M ARR potential from three contracts.
  • UK corporate wellness: FTSE 350 companies average 4,200 employees. At £15/employee/month across senior employees (estimated 20% uptake at a 1,000-person firm): £36k/month per corporate client. Twenty clients: £8.6M ARR.
  • UK private healthcare/insurance: 4.1M people have private medical insurance (ABI 2022). Insurers seeking digital health interventions to reduce claims. This is a 3–5 year opportunity, not a current one.

Geography

UK first for 24 months. Australia in year three — cultural affinity, similar income profile, strong fitness culture, the founding team has Australian market knowledge. US in year four — larger market, much higher CAC, requires either a separate fundraise or a partner with existing distribution.

Section 03

Competitive Landscape — Brutally Honest

MyFitnessPal

150M+ registered users, acquired by Francisco Partners 2020. Dominant in calorie tracking. Database of 14M+ foods, strong network effects, habit formation through streaks. What it does badly: the "explain why" layer is entirely absent. Every recommendation is a number, never a mechanism. The user who wants to understand their biology is not served. Where Soma wins: every user who has used MFP and felt like a data entry clerk rather than someone being coached. Where Soma loses: food database depth, brand recognition, free tier pricing, mobile UX maturity. Hard to overcome: 150M users and 15 years of brand equity in calorie tracking.

Whoop

Series F, $3.6B valuation. Best-in-class wearable recovery data. Genuine HRV, sleep staging, strain measurement. The hardware moat is real. What it does badly: the coaching layer is still generic. Knowing your recovery score is not the same as knowing why it's low and what to do about it specifically. Where Soma wins: users who have Whoop data but no framework for acting on it. Where Soma loses: hardware integration (Whoop owns the data at source), brand prestige, the $30/month price anchoring that makes premium positioning easier. Critical threat: if Whoop builds a serious AI coaching layer on top of their hardware data, the combination is formidable. Their recent moves suggest they are trying.

Caliber

Series A, ~$10M raised. Closest direct competitor in the "AI personalised coaching" space. Real coach access, good personalisation, strong retention among users who engage. What it does badly: the human coach model creates unit economics that cap scalability. CAC is high, coach capacity limits growth, price point (£50–150/month) restricts TAM. Where Soma wins: price point accessibility, science explanation layer, no waitlist. Where Soma loses: human coach warmth and accountability, brand credibility in the PT community.

Zoe

Series B, ~£25M raised, UK. Personalised nutrition based on gut microbiome and blood glucose testing. Genuine science credibility, strong brand narrative, media presence. £299 for the test kit limits mass market reach. Narrow focus on nutrition/gut health, no training layer. Where Soma wins: training integration, breadth of personalisation, price accessibility. Where Soma loses: Zoe's scientific credibility is established and funded; the Tim Spector association is a brand asset that took years and capital to build. This is the competitor to watch most carefully — they could expand into the full Soma proposition.

Levels

US, ~$50M raised. Continuous glucose monitoring for metabolic health. Data depth, credibility with high-performance audience, strong content. Requires hardware (CGM), expensive, US-only. Not a current UK competitor but a signal of the premium personalised health direction the market is moving.

Noom

~$400M revenue, pre-IPO. Psychology-based weight loss. Behaviour change framework, large scale, clinical study backing. What it does badly: the product experience has been consistently criticised as repetitive and low-quality; retention is reportedly poor after the initial programme. Not the same audience — Noom targets weight loss motivation, Soma targets performance understanding.

Apple Health / Google Fit — the real long-term threat

This deserves direct treatment because it is routinely underweighted. Apple has Health Records, HealthKit, the Watch sensor stack, and more iOS users than any fitness app. They have stated publicly that health is Apple's greatest contribution to humanity. When Apple ships an AI health coach natively into iOS, every third-party app that relies on Apple device users faces an existential question. The timeline is uncertain but not implausible within 5 years.

The mitigation: Soma's moat must be the science architecture and accumulated personalisation data, not the interface. A better explanation of human biology is defensible even if Apple builds a competent coach. A better UI is not.

Section 04

The Category Thesis

The argument is this: there is a category of product that does not yet exist at consumer scale — call it personalised health intelligence — defined as systems that take individual biological data and translate it into specific, mechanistically explained guidance. Not recommendations. Explanations. The user understands not just what to do but why their body specifically responds to training, nutrition, and sleep the way it does.

This is meaningfully different from fitness tracking (what did you do), personalised plans (what should you do), and generic health content (what is generally true). The combination of specificity and explanation at the individual level is the category.

Why it's real and durable

The underlying human need — wanting to understand your own body rather than just follow instructions — is not going away. As education levels rise, as institutional trust in generic health advice falls, and as the consequences of poor health decisions compound through midlife, the demand for intelligent, specific, honest health guidance grows. The science is also getting better: the mechanisms of cortisol, mTOR, GLP-1, sleep architecture, visceral fat accumulation are increasingly well understood and increasingly specific to demographic variables. The explanation layer gets richer over time, not thinner.

Why it requires this specific combination

Without the biology knowledge, AI generates confident-sounding generic content. Without AI, personalisation at scale is economically impossible. Without personalisation, the science content is generic health information, of which there is already an overwhelming supply. The intersection of all three is where the category lives, and it requires a founder who understands biology at depth — which is the rare constraint that makes this defensible.

The steelman against

The most serious counter-argument is not that the category doesn't exist — it's that users don't want what it offers. Most people who download a fitness app want to lose weight. They want a number to go down. They do not want to understand mTOR. They want someone to tell them what to eat and for it to work. The research on health behaviour change consistently shows that knowledge does not predict behaviour. People who understand the mechanism of sleep deprivation on testosterone still watch Netflix until 1am.

This is a real risk. The product must be designed so that the explanation is in service of action, not a substitute for it. The coach voice must be prescriptive first, explanatory second. "Eat this now" before "here's why this matters." If the science layer becomes a reason to feel educated without changing behaviour, it is noise rather than signal.

Section 05

Blue Sky Vision — 10 Years

"If the consumer product works, the retention is proven, and the B2B layer is operating, the question becomes: what is the largest version of this idea?"

At ten years, Soma is not a fitness app. It is personalised health infrastructure — the operating system for how serious individuals understand and manage their biological performance across their whole life.

Three directions simultaneously

The consumer layer has deepened from daily plans and nutrition into longitudinal health management — tracking how your body changes across years, flagging biological inflection points (the metabolic shift at 40, the hormonal changes of perimenopause, the cardiovascular risk accumulation that's invisible to a 35-year-old), and connecting those patterns to the daily decisions that compound them. This is not healthcare. It is the intelligent layer between what a GP measures annually and what you do every day.

The enterprise layer has become a category. Organisations — corporations, sports teams, professional services firms, military — pay for Soma as human performance infrastructure. Not wellness as a perk but cognitive and physical optimisation as operational necessity. The insight that a senior fund manager's decision quality degrades measurably with sleep deprivation, poor nutrition timing, and high cortisol load — and that this is measurable and correctable — is worth institutional-grade pricing.

The data layer has become the most valuable asset. A decade of longitudinal data from hundreds of thousands of individuals — daily nutrition, training load, sleep quality, body composition, hormonal indicators, cognitive performance proxies — represents something that does not exist anywhere. Not in clinical databases (which are episodic), not in wearable data (which lacks contextual richness), not in research studies (which are short-duration). This dataset is the basis for research partnerships, insurance risk modelling, pharmaceutical trial recruitment, and population health insights at a granularity that changes what is scientifically possible.

The category it defines

Informed human performance management. The world that looks different: one in which the gap between what elite athletes and their support teams know about human biology, and what an intelligent 40-year-old professional knows about their own body, has substantially closed. Not because everyone has a team of nutritionists and coaches — but because the knowledge that those teams carry has been made accessible, affordable, and personalised.

Business Model Architecture

Model 1: D2C Subscription

Pricing: Single tier at £39/month or £299/year. Free/curious tier dropped for now — paying users give honest feedback that free users never will.

Unit economics: Monthly churn target 6%. Average subscriber lifetime: 16.7 months. LTV = £39 × 16.7 = £651. Annual plan £299 upfront — at 40% annual take-up, improves cash flow significantly and extends effective lifetime.

CAC targets: Content/organic channel £15–25 per subscriber. Professional referral channel £20–35. Paid social (if deployed in year 2) £45–80. Target blended CAC: £25–35. Payback period: under 1 month at £39 pricing. This is genuinely good economics if achieved.

  • Best case: Content engine compounds, referrals activate, churn at 5–6%. 12,000 subscribers by month 24, £2.2M ARR.
  • Base case: Slower content traction, churn at 7–8%, 8,000 subscribers, £1.5M ARR.
  • Worst case: Content doesn't rank, paid social required, CAC rises to £60+, churn at 10%. 4,000 subscribers, £720k ARR. Economics break at this level.

Model 2: White Label Licensing

Who buys: National gym chains (PureGym, David Lloyd, Nuffield), private healthcare providers (Bupa, Nuffield Health), insurance companies building digital health propositions (Vitality, AXA Health).

Pricing: £3–5 per active member per month. "Active" defined as logged in within 30 days. Minimum contract: £10k/month. Typical contract: £25–75k/month for a mid-size gym chain with 10,000–25,000 active digitally-engaged members.

Sales cycle: 6–18 months. Enterprise health contracts require procurement, legal, data protection review (GDPR), IT integration assessment, pilot period. The first contract will take longer than subsequent ones. The first contract you will probably discount or structure as a pilot to get the case study — that case study is worth more than the contract revenue.

Model 3: Corporate Wellness

Buyer persona: HR Directors and Chief People Officers at knowledge businesses — financial services, law, consulting, tech. Post-COVID, wellness budget exists and is being spent badly. Decision maker is typically CHRO or HR Director. Champion is often a senior individual contributor who uses the product personally.

Pricing: £15–25 per employee per month for a defined cohort. Not whole-company rollout initially — start with the top 100–200 employees. A 500-person financial services firm at £20/person/month for 150 senior employees: £36k/month, £432k/year.

The pitch: Decision quality costs money. A senior partner operating at 80% cognitive capacity because of sleep deprivation and poor nutrition timing makes worse decisions. The institutional finance background is the credibility to make this argument to people who would dismiss it from a wellness startup.

Model 4: Data and Insights

What data: Longitudinal individual health behaviour data — daily nutrition, training load, sleep patterns, body composition changes, supplement usage, hormonal indicators (inferred), stress indicators, cognitive performance proxies. At 50,000+ users, this is genuinely novel.

Who buys: Academic research institutions. Pharmaceutical companies (trial recruitment, real-world evidence). Food and supplement manufacturers (ingredient efficacy in real-world conditions). Health insurance actuaries (risk modelling).

Privacy framework: Explicit opt-in consent required. Data is aggregated and anonymised before any third-party access. Individual-level data never sold. GDPR compliance built from the start, not retrofitted.

When viable: Year 3–4, minimum 30,000 users. Year 4 realistic contribution: £500k–1.5M ARR.

Section 11

Retention Engineering

The category average for fitness app monthly churn is 8–12% (Sensor Tower, Appsflyer benchmarks 2022–23). At 10% monthly churn, mean subscriber lifetime is 10 months; LTV at £14.99/month is £150. This is too low for the unit economics to work at any reasonable CAC. This is the most important operational section in this document.

What drives churn at this price point

Health subscriptions churn when the product stops feeling necessary — not when it stops working. The user who loses 2kg and feels good cancels because they've "done it." The user who feels like the product genuinely knows them, and that leaving means losing something irreplaceable, does not cancel. The entire retention strategy must be oriented toward creating the second kind of user, not just delivering results to the first.

90-day retention drivers

Weekly review — the highest-leverage single feature. A personalised narrative debrief every Sunday that references specific events from the user's week: "you hit protein four of seven days; the misses were both Tuesday evening and Friday — that's a pattern worth addressing." A weekly ritual that is difficult to replicate and meaningful enough to drive the Sunday open. Must be live before significant acquisition spend begins.

Progressive personalisation. The plan in month three must be visibly different from month one because it actually knows you better. The coach must explicitly note why: "your body scan changed your protein targets; the plan updated accordingly." Without this, users who have good early results plateau in perceived value.

12-month retention drivers

Coach memory. Every conversation, every SOS moment, every deviation logged accumulates into a picture the coach explicitly references. "You mentioned last week that evening meals were hardest — this week's plan adjusted for that." That's not a feature. That's a relationship. Relationships are sticky. Supabase syncs 12 data types across devices; the weekly reconciliation pass (logged data changing future plans) is now live as of Rev 5.

Body scan loop. Every time someone scans and their numbers change, targets recalculate and the plan shifts. Creates a reason to scan regularly, which creates a habit loop, which creates retention. Users who scan regularly are measuring progress; measuring progress is the strongest predictor of continued engagement.

3-year retention drivers

Data accumulation moat. Leaving Soma means losing years of data and the personalisation built on it. The accumulated understanding of how this specific person responds to training, nutrition, and sleep — their patterns, their triggers, their optimal timing — is irreplaceable. This is why progressive personalisation isn't just a retention feature: it's the data moat that makes the product indispensable over a long enough time horizon.

Identity integration. The user who thinks of themselves as "someone who trains consistently, eats with intention, and manages recovery" — not just someone who uses Soma — has integrated the product into their identity. Identity-based engagement is the most durable form of retention. The coach voice, the science explanations, the weekly review — all of these should reinforce the user's identity as someone who takes their biology seriously, not just someone pursuing a goal.

The honest statement

Soma does not yet know its true retention numbers because there are no users with six-month+ tenure in a persistent, tracked product. The foundation phase is partly about creating the conditions to measure this properly. Everything else in this document is projection until that data exists.

Section 12

Go-to-Market Strategy

Organic search / SEO — most important channel

The science content layer is already built and is better than most of what ranks for relevant terms. The mechanism: publish all science pages with proper SEO (title tags, meta descriptions, schema, internal linking, fast load), create cluster content around each topic (one pillar piece on cortisol, five cluster pieces on cortisol-related topics), build backlinks through journalist outreach and professional citations. Timeline: 6–9 months to meaningful ranking. CAC: £15–25. Scalability ceiling: very high — SEO compounds indefinitely. Seamus's primary job in months 4–12.

Professional referrals — second most important, most underappreciated

A personal trainer who recommends Soma to 10 clients per month is worth £3,900/month in subscriber revenue at zero additional marketing cost. The pitch to PTs: "Soma explains the science behind what you're prescribing. Your clients understand why the programme works, which improves adherence. You get the credit." Build a referral portal — trackable links, 10–15% commission, co-branded landing page for each referrer. CAC: £20–35. Referred users have social proof and convert at higher rates with lower churn.

Section 13

The Moat — What Actually Defends This

Moat 1: Science architecture as brand

How real it is: Real, but slower to build than it feels. The science layer currently exists as product content. To become a genuine brand moat — something people cite when they recommend Soma, a reason journalists cover it, a reason professionals associate with it — it needs public credibility signals. Academic citations, professional endorsements, media coverage that references the science quality.

How easily replicated: A funded competitor could hire a team of sports scientists and nutritionists and replicate the science architecture in 12–18 months. What they cannot replicate quickly: the tone, the depth of integration with the coaching layer, and the personalisation logic that determines which science matters for which individual. Timeline to establish meaningful brand-level science credibility: 18–36 months.

Moat 2: Accumulated personalisation data

How real it is: Potentially the strongest moat but currently does not exist. The value is directly proportional to the number of users with long tenure. At 10,000 subscribers with 12-month average tenure, you have a dataset that begins to have real value. At 50,000, it is genuinely defensible.

The key insight: Getting there requires keeping subscribers for long enough — which is the retention problem. The data moat and the retention problem are the same problem. Fix retention and the moat builds automatically.

Moat 3: Trust and credibility

How real it is: The most durable moat and the slowest to build. Trust in health products is won over years and lost in days. The honest acknowledgment of uncertainty builds more trust than confident claims, even when it feels commercially risky.

Why it might be weaker than it appears: The most dangerous version is that the science layer is genuinely valuable to the founder because the founder finds biology fascinating — but the target user values explanation less than results. If most users will switch to a product that generates equivalent results with less explanation, the science architecture is an asset with a narrower audience than assumed. Track whether users who engage with the science content retain better than those who don't. If the correlation is weak, adjust the product strategy accordingly.
