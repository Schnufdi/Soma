Insulin sensitivity — the central metabolic variable
Insulin sensitivity determines how effectively your cells respond to insulin's signal to absorb glucose. High sensitivity means small amounts of insulin efficiently clear glucose. Low sensitivity (insulin resistance) means the pancreas must produce more and more insulin to achieve the same clearance — until eventually it cannot keep up. This trajectory is the precursor to type 2 diabetes, but the metabolic dysfunction begins 10-15 years before a clinical diagnosis.
1
Established
The mechanism of insulin resistance
Muscle is the primary site of insulin-mediated glucose disposal — approximately 75% of a glucose load is taken up by skeletal muscle. When muscle cells accumulate intramyocellular lipid (fat inside muscle cells — caused by excess caloric intake, particularly from refined carbohydrates and saturated fat combined), the insulin signalling cascade is disrupted. The GLUT4 transporter — the door that insulin opens to let glucose into the cell — fails to translocate to the cell membrane in adequate numbers. The cell is not broken. The lock-and-key mechanism is working. But the door is stuck. The consequence is elevated fasting glucose, hyperinsulinaemia, and progressive metabolic dysfunction.
2
Established
Muscle mass as metabolic protection
Every kilogram of skeletal muscle is a glucose sink — it absorbs glucose during and after exercise via both insulin-dependent and insulin-independent pathways. This is why resistance training is the most powerful intervention for metabolic health available: it increases the size and metabolic capacity of the primary glucose-clearing tissue. A person with greater muscle mass can eat more carbohydrates with less glucose variability and lower insulin requirement — because they have more disposal capacity. The progressive loss of muscle mass from the mid-30s (sarcopenia) is a primary driver of declining metabolic health with age, independent of changes in diet.
3
Established
Biomarkers to track — what your GP measures and what they miss
Standard NHS checks typically include fasting glucose and HbA1c — both useful but late indicators. By the time HbA1c rises above normal, insulin resistance has typically been present for 5-10 years. Earlier indicators include: fasting insulin (elevated years before glucose rises — your pancreas compensating successfully, but at cost), triglycerides (elevated TG reflects insulin resistance-driven hepatic lipogenesis), HDL cholesterol (low HDL accompanies insulin resistance), and waist circumference (visceral fat is the most metabolically active fat depot and the strongest clinical proxy for insulin resistance). A HOMA-IR score (fasting glucose × fasting insulin ÷ 22.5) below 1.0 is optimal; above 2.0 indicates significant insulin resistance.

Metabolic flexibility — the ability to switch fuels
Metabolic flexibility is the capacity to efficiently switch between glucose and fat as a fuel source depending on availability and demand. It is the opposite of being metabolically "stuck" — dependent on glucose, unable to access fat stores efficiently, producing energy crashes when blood sugar drops. It is one of the most underappreciated markers of metabolic health.
1
Established
What metabolic inflexibility looks and feels like
A metabolically inflexible person cannot efficiently oxidise fat when glucose is unavailable. The practical consequences: energy crashes 3-4 hours after eating, inability to skip a meal without brain fog and irritability, inability to train effectively in a fasted state, poor fat loss despite caloric restriction (the body resists accessing fat stores), and afternoon energy dependency on caffeine and carbohydrates. None of these are character failures. They are physiological consequences of chronically high insulin levels preventing fat oxidation. High insulin — even when glucose is normal — directly inhibits the hormone-sensitive lipase enzyme that releases stored fat for energy.
2
Established
Zone 2 training and the fat oxidation machinery
Zone 2 exercise (60-70% maximum heart rate — a pace you can hold a conversation) primarily uses fat as fuel and specifically drives mitochondrial biogenesis — the creation of new mitochondria — in slow-twitch type I muscle fibres. These fibres have the highest fat oxidation capacity. Zone 2 is the primary training stimulus for rebuilding the fat oxidation machinery. Peter Attia's protocol — 3-4 hours per week of Zone 2 — produces measurable improvements in fat oxidation rate within 8-12 weeks. The metabolic flexibility this builds is not just a performance benefit: it is protective against metabolic disease, insulin resistance, and cognitive decline associated with impaired glucose metabolism.
3
Emerging evidence
Time-restricted eating and insulin sensitivity
Compressing eating to an 8-10 hour window — particularly earlier in the day — has emerging evidence for improving insulin sensitivity independent of caloric restriction. The mechanism may involve circadian alignment of insulin signalling (insulin sensitivity is higher in the morning due to circadian clock gene expression in metabolic tissues) and longer fasting periods allowing insulin to fall low enough to permit fat oxidation. The evidence is not yet as robust as Zone 2 training for improving metabolic flexibility — several RCTs show the effect is primarily mediated by mild caloric restriction rather than timing per se. But aligning eating windows with morning/midday circadian peaks has plausible mechanistic support beyond simple restriction.

Glucose management — practical control levers
Glucose variability — the spikes and crashes — is addressable without extreme dietary restriction. The interventions with the strongest evidence work by slowing glucose absorption, increasing disposal capacity, and timing intake to match insulin sensitivity. Most people see 30-50% reductions in postprandial spikes within two weeks of consistent implementation.
1
Established
Food order and the glucose spike — a simple, high-impact lever
Eating fibre and protein before carbohydrates at a meal significantly reduces the postprandial glucose spike — by 36-57% in RCT data (Shukla et al., Diabetes Care). The mechanism is physical and enzymatic: fibre forms a viscous layer in the gut that slows glucose absorption. Protein triggers incretin hormones that slow gastric emptying and stimulate insulin before glucose arrives. The food is the same. The order changes everything. In practice: eat vegetables first, protein second, carbohydrates last. A salad starter before pasta produces a dramatically different glucose profile than pasta first. This requires no dietary restriction — only sequencing.
2
Established
Post-meal walking — the fastest glucose disposal tool available
A 10-minute walk after eating activates GLUT4 translocation in muscle via an insulin-independent pathway — the mechanical contraction of muscle directly transports glucose into cells without requiring insulin signalling. Multiple RCTs confirm a 20-30% reduction in postprandial glucose peak from a 10-minute walk begun within 15 minutes of finishing a meal. The effect is superior to the same walk taken before the meal. This is why post-meal walking is one of the highest-leverage, lowest-cost metabolic interventions available — it requires no dietary change, no equipment, and produces measurable glucose benefit from the first walk. Three post-meal walks per day produces Zone 2-equivalent glucose control improvements over weeks.
3
Established
Resistance training and 24-hour glucose management
A single resistance training session improves insulin sensitivity for 24-48 hours post-exercise — the "afterburn" is not primarily caloric, it is metabolic. Muscle glycogen depleted during training creates a glucose sink: carbohydrates consumed post-training are prioritised for glycogen resynthesis rather than fat storage. Training on 4 days per week versus 2 measurably improves weekly glucose control, with additive effects from cardio. The combination of resistance training (GLUT4 upregulation), cardio (mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation), and post-meal walks (insulin-independent GLUT4 activation) represents the complete mechanical glucose control toolkit.

Metabolic ageing — what shifts from 35 onwards
Metabolic health does not decline uniformly with age. Several specific, measurable changes accelerate between 35 and 55. Understanding the mechanism makes the intervention obvious — and makes the decline optional rather than inevitable.
1
Established
Muscle loss (sarcopenia) — the primary metabolic ageing driver
Without deliberate resistance training, adults lose 3-5% of muscle mass per decade from age 30. From 50 onwards, the rate accelerates. As muscle mass falls, glucose disposal capacity falls with it — producing declining insulin sensitivity that appears to be "ageing" but is actually disuse. The trajectory is not a one-way street: resistance training at any age increases muscle mass, GLUT4 expression, and insulin sensitivity. Older adults who begin resistance training show metabolic improvements equivalent to people 10-15 years younger. The intervention is resistance training — heavy enough to stimulate hypertrophy (RPE 7-9), 3-4 sessions per week, consistently sustained.
2
Established
Visceral fat accumulation — the metabolic toxin
Visceral fat — fat stored around the abdominal organs, not subcutaneous fat — is metabolically active in a harmful way. It secretes pro-inflammatory cytokines (particularly TNF-α and IL-6) and free fatty acids directly into the portal circulation, impairing hepatic insulin signalling and driving systemic insulin resistance. Waist circumference is the most accessible proxy for visceral fat accumulation and is a stronger predictor of metabolic disease than BMI. Testosterone decline in men (1-2% per year from 30) and oestrogen decline in women (accelerating from perimenopause) both shift fat distribution toward visceral deposition — which is why abdominal fat accumulation in 40-50 year olds often follows hormonal change rather than dietary change.
3
Emerging evidence
Mitochondrial decline and the energy-ageing connection
Mitochondrial number and function decline with age — a process accelerated by sedentary behaviour and attenuated by exercise. The consequence is reduced ATP production capacity, impaired fat oxidation, and lower metabolic rate at rest. Emerging evidence suggests this mitochondrial ageing is one of the upstream drivers of insulin resistance rather than a downstream consequence. Zone 2 exercise (the primary stimulus for mitochondrial biogenesis) may be the most age-protective metabolic intervention available — Peter Attia refers to it as the "exercise prescription for metabolic health" in 40+ year olds specifically. The data on longevity and metabolic health from high Zone 2 training volume are among the most consistent in exercise science.

Levers — ranked by evidence and implementation ease
The metabolic flexibility levers with the strongest evidence do not require dietary perfection. They work through physiology — training the glucose disposal machinery, reducing postprandial spikes, and gradually improving the body's ability to switch between fuels. Each layer is additive.
💪 Resistance training — 3-4×/week, RPE 7-9
The single most powerful metabolic intervention. Increases muscle mass (the primary glucose sink), upregulates GLUT4 expression, and improves insulin sensitivity for 24-48h post-session. More muscle = more metabolic protection. Non-negotiable for 35+ metabolic health.
🚶 Post-meal walks — 10 min within 15 min of eating
Activates insulin-independent GLUT4 translocation. Reduces postprandial glucose by 20-30%. Requires no equipment. Cumulative effect on insulin sensitivity equals Zone 2 over weeks. The highest ROI daily habit available for metabolic health.
🥗 Food order — fibre + protein before carbs
Reduces postprandial glucose spike 36-57% with no dietary restriction. Fibre slows glucose absorption. Protein triggers incretins before glucose arrives. Eat the salad and protein first. Then the carbs. Same food, radically different glucose profile.
🏃 Zone 2 cardio — 3-4h/week at 60-70% max HR
Primary driver of mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation capacity. Builds the metabolic flexibility to switch fuel sources efficiently. Requires 8-12 weeks of consistent Zone 2 before measurable improvements in fat oxidation rate appear. This is the long-game metabolic investment.
🕐 Eating window — compress to 8-10h, morning-weighted
Allows insulin to fall to fat-oxidation-permitting levels overnight. Morning-weighted eating aligns with circadian peaks in insulin sensitivity. Effect partially mediated by mild caloric restriction but timing has additional mechanistic support. Start with a clear 3h pre-sleep cutoff.
😴 Sleep — 7-9h, consistent timing
A single night of 4-5h sleep reduces insulin sensitivity by 25% in healthy adults. Chronic sleep restriction produces metabolic changes comparable to significant weight gain. Sleep is not a passive metabolic event — it is active metabolic restoration. The other levers work less well on inadequate sleep.
