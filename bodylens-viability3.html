Brutally honest verdict — Revision 4
Brand, positioning, and pricing are now clear.
The intelligence gap is still the one thing that matters.
This revision closes the surface gaps that would have undermined trust at first contact: the product now has a distinct identity (Soma — not a name collision with bodylens.com), a public-accessible legal foundation, a clear pricing structure positioned against PT coaching rather than consumer trackers, and a meal planning workflow that reflects how people actually prepare food. The reshuffle logic bug — a genuine correctness failure — has been fixed, along with the destination-day cache invalidation and the getMissed() regression that followed it. A global glossary system and prep day tab have been added to the meal planner surface.

What the product still lacks is unchanged from Revision 3: the feedback loop. A user who trains with Soma for 12 weeks has given the system 84 day logs, 12 weekly ledger entries, and a detailed strength progression record — none of which currently changes what the plan recommends next week. The data layer is built, Supabase is syncing, the infrastructure is complete. The weekly reconciliation pass is the single most important thing left to build. Everything else in this product is ahead of the competition. That piece determines whether Soma is genuinely adaptive or a very sophisticated data entry tool.

Built — Brand & Positioning
Soma — full rebrand across 59 files, distinct market position
BodyLens was a name collision — bodylens.com is a dark-theme, teal-accent fitness product with consumer "Warrior/Champion" pricing tiers. Soma (σῶμα — Greek for "body", used in neuroscience, biology, and philosophy) is clean, distinct, and owns a different register. 59 files updated: all title tags, nav brand, manifest.json, legal pages, JS strings, footer references. File names kept as bodylens-*.html to avoid link rot — the rename is entirely in the visible layer. localStorage keys (bl_ prefix) unchanged — they're invisible to users. The brand change took one multi-agent batch pass; zero link breakage.
Built — Commercial
Pricing section on landing page — £39/month, £299/year, PT framing
Previous landing page had no pricing. Pricing is now defined and public: £39/month or £299/year (saves £169). Single tier — not a Warrior/Champion consumer model. The positioning callout: "The cost of one PT session — for a full month of structured intelligence." PT sessions run £40–80; one month of Soma is the same price and delivers daily AI-generated plans, adaptive coaching, full nutrition, recovery, and supplementation intelligence. This removes the consumer tracker price comparison entirely and benchmarks against the closest human equivalent. Monthly card (outline) + Annual card (jade fill, "best value" badge). Beta pricing note: early users lock in the rate permanently. Early cohort incentive without a discount code.
Built — Legal & Trust
Terms and Privacy pages publicly accessible — no auth required
Both legal pages previously loaded nav.js and supabase-auth.js, which redirected unauthenticated users to the login page. A user evaluating whether to sign up — the exact person who needs to read the Privacy Policy — couldn't access it. Scripts removed from both pages, replaced with a self-contained sticky header using javascript:history.back(). No auth dependency. Terms and Privacy are now always accessible. This is a legal requirement in most jurisdictions and a trust signal at exactly the evaluation moment that matters.
Built — Meal Planning
Prep day tab — AI-generated weekly batch cook plan
The meal planner previously showed Daily, Weekly, and Shopping views. Most users don't want to cook every day — they want to prep once and assemble through the week. Prep Day tab added: sends weekly meal summary to Claude, returns a structured JSON plan: prepDay, totalPrepTime, proteins, carbs, veg, sauces, order (step-by-step sequence), containers, tip. Displayed as ingredient cards with name/amount/method/store-as, plus a numbered cook order and contextual tip. Cached under bl_meals_prep_v1; Regenerate button clears cache. No competitor has an AI prep day generator. This closes a real behavioural gap between "here's a meal plan" and "here's how to actually execute it."
Built — Knowledge Layer
Global glossary system — bl-glossary.js, 60+ defined terms
bl-glossary.js provides an in-context terminology layer across the entire product. Any defined term — RPE, macros, progressive overload, zone 2, mTOR, HRV — renders as a dotted underline; one tap opens a tooltip with the definition, without leaving the current view. Terms are defined once and applied consistently across pages via a single include. Removes the gap between a sophisticated AI output and a user who doesn't know what RIR or deload means. This is the kind of feature that makes the product feel like it was built for people who want to learn, not just follow instructions.
Built — Meals Redesign
Macro snapshot header, calorie distribution, visual coherence
The meals page previously showed a list of meals without summary-level nutrition context. Redesigned with a macro snapshot header showing daily totals (calories, protein, carbs, fat) and a calorie distribution chart showing meal-by-meal breakdown. Calorie distribution bug fixed: the previous implementation miscalculated per-meal percentages when regenerating plans. The visual hierarchy now leads with the nutritional story, then the individual meals. Consistent with the daily plan's energy balance section — the product now has a coherent nutrition data narrative across both surfaces.
Built — Weekly Plan
Exercise detail expansion, cardio intensity hints, energy balance
Weekly plan cards now expand to show full exercise detail: all working sets with load, reps, and rest, rather than a headline summary. Cardio blocks show intensity guidance (heart rate zone, perceived exertion target). Energy balance section added, showing the net caloric picture for training days vs rest days. Admin preview login allows testing the full plan flow without using the production Supabase instance — critical for development quality assurance.
Fixed — Scheduling Logic
Reshuffle getMissed() correctness — vacated-day edge case
The reshuffle feature writes a {priority:'rest', source:'reshuffle-vacated'} override to the source day when a session is moved. This caused a regression: getMissed() called getEffDay() which returned the merged override with priority:'rest', so the original training session was no longer detected as missed even if the reshuffled destination day hadn't been trained either. Fix: both getMissed() (weekly plan) and getMissedSessions() (daily plan) now read p.weekPlan[i].priority (original, pre-override) for the training check, then check override entries specifically for reshuffle-vacated source to determine whether the session was legitimately rescheduled and completed at the destination. Destination-day cache clearing also added to applyReshuffle() — previously only today's cache was cleared, leaving the destination day with a stale cached plan.
Fixed — Infrastructure
Service worker network-first cache — Safari staleness eliminated
Service worker updated to network-first strategy with cache fallback. Previously the SW served stale cached versions of JS modules when the network was available, causing freshly deployed code to not reach Safari users. Now: network is always attempted first; cache is the fallback for offline/slow connections only. Combined with the Revision 3 vercel.json no-cache headers, this closes both the CDN-level and service-worker-level Safari caching gaps. Cache versioning ensures stale entries are pruned on each new deploy.
03 Critical gaps — current state
What remains
genuinely thin.
Surface gaps and trust gaps have largely closed across four revisions. What remains is a single architectural gap and two important but lower-severity items.
Unchanged — The defining gap
The feedback loop still doesn't exist
84 day logs. 12 week ledger entries. Strength progression records. Sleep patterns. Energy ratings. RPE trends. None of this changes what the plan recommends next week. The data is captured, synced to Supabase, and stored perfectly. It is not being reasoned about across time. A user who trains for three months with Soma gets the same programme logic on week 12 as week 1 — unless they manually regenerate. This is the architectural difference between a sophisticated data entry tool and an adaptive coaching system. Every piece of infrastructure needed to build this exists. The weekly reconciliation pass — a single Claude call reading the last 7 day logs and generating programme adaptations — is unbuilt. It is the highest-leverage thing left to build in this product.
Urgent — pre-launch blocker
Auth gate — all pages still accessible without login
The authentication infrastructure is fully built and working. The gate is not enforced. Any URL in the app is accessible without a Supabase session. This was acceptable for development; it is a blocker before public launch with paying users. One redirect check in each page's init sequence. The pattern exists in auth-protected pages — needs rolling out systematically. Must ship before any paid onboarding begins.
Partially addressed
Science library evidence audit — still unstarted
The evidence tier framework is live on accelerators and supplements. The science library (15+ pages covering sleep, gut health, longevity, hormones, metabolism) predates the framework and hasn't been audited. Individual pages still make claims with more certainty than the research supports. A systematic pass applying the same tier methodology — adding evidence badges and updating coaching instructions — is the remaining scientific rigour work. Medium effort, high trust impact for the sophisticated user segment Soma is positioned for.

05 The remaining gap
The gap between
impressive and elite.
The trust infrastructure is now strong. The product surface is broad, honest, and commercially positioned. The differentiating investment is entirely in the reasoning engine — which the data layer exists to power.
Architecture needed — highest priority
The weekly reconciliation pass — logged data must change the programme
Every piece of infrastructure needed to build this exists. Day logs are in Supabase with trainStatus, exercises, energy, sleep, and RPE. Week ledger tracks the full week. The profile holds the programme and weekPlan. What's missing is the reasoning pass — a weekly Claude call reading the last 7 days of logs, identifying patterns (consistent low energy, missed sessions, RPE mismatches), and outputting programme adaptations or coaching interventions. This writes back to profile.behaviourMemory and surfaces a weekly coaching summary. Cost: ~$0.03/week per user. This is the highest-leverage thing left to build — it closes the most important intelligence gap and is the architectural difference between a data capture tool and an adaptive coach.
Architecture needed
Multi-week context in programme generation
The week ledger exists in Supabase. It is not passed into the daily plan generation context. Passing the last 4 weeks of weekly summaries into the programme prompt would enable genuine periodisation reasoning: deload detection from fatigue patterns, volume progression recommendations, session timing adjustments based on actual recovery. This is a data retrieval problem the infrastructure now supports — not an architectural build, just a prompt injection change in buildDay().
Architecture needed
Science library evidence audit
The evidence tier framework is live on accelerators and supplements. The science library (15+ pages covering sleep, gut health, longevity, hormones, HRV, metabolism) predates the framework and hasn't been audited. Individual pages make claims with more certainty than the research supports. Applying the same tier methodology systematically — adding evidence badges to each science claim and updating the coaching instructions to reflect the science library's content — is the remaining scientific rigour work. Medium effort, high trust impact for sophisticated users.

07 Differentiation — Revision 4
What makes it
defensible now.
The original assessment said differentiation was real but fragile. That has changed substantially across four revisions — the moat is now structural, not just aesthetic.
New moat — brand & pricing position
Soma is positioned against PT coaching, not consumer trackers
bodylens.com's Warrior/Champion tiers (£12.99/£24.99) frame that product as a premium consumer tracker. Soma at £39/month benchmarks against a PT session — an entirely different reference class. The user who is choosing between Soma and a personal trainer is a different, more motivated, higher-value user than the one choosing between apps. The PT comparison shifts the perceived value proposition: not "should I pay for another app" but "should I hire a trainer or use Soma." One PT session = one month of daily structured intelligence. This is a genuine positioning advantage, not a price point decision. And because no competitor is making this comparison, it's uncontested.
Existing moat — epistemic honesty
No competitor labels claims by evidence quality
The four-tier evidence framework remains genuinely differentiating. Every major fitness product — Whoop, Future, MyFitnessPal, Levels — presents recommendations with uniform confidence regardless of the underlying evidence. Soma is the only consumer fitness product that labels claims as Established, Emerging, Limited, or Anecdotal and tells users why. For a sophisticated user — a doctor, sports scientist, serious athlete — this is the difference between trusting the product and dismissing it. The calibration is real: creatine, zone 2, and sleep extension get full confidence; fadogia agrestis gets an explicit animal-study-only warning. That honesty is harder to replicate than a feature.
Existing moat — clinical safety posture
Risk detection is clinically unusual for consumer fitness
No consumer fitness app has a working risk classifier that modifies AI coaching behaviour based on detected health conditions. A user with hypertension gets different intensity guidance. A user with disordered eating history doesn't receive caloric deficit prescriptions. A user with a herniated disc doesn't receive spinal loading recommendations. This is the kind of safety posture that matters at scale — and it's structural, not just a disclaimer.
Structural advantage
The operating system positioning remains unoccupied
Training, nutrition, sleep, recovery, supplementation, body intelligence, science education, meal prep planning, and coaching in a single coherent product — under a brand (Soma) that is distinct, ownable, and semantically appropriate. No competitor occupies this position credibly. Whoop is recovery data. Future is human coaching. Levels is metabolic health. Soma is the only product attempting to build an intelligent OS for how a person relates to their body across all dimensions simultaneously. The gap between what Soma is now and what it becomes when the feedback loop is running is the clearest opportunity in the space.
