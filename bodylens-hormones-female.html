The menstrual cycle — a performance variable that is almost universally ignored
A typical 28-day cycle is not hormonally uniform. Oestrogen, progesterone, testosterone, FSH, and LH all rise and fall in a specific, predictable pattern — and each shift has measurable consequences for muscle protein synthesis, fat metabolism, substrate utilisation during exercise, recovery rate, thermoregulation, injury risk, and caloric needs. Training programmes that ignore this are optimising for men.
1
Established
The four phases — what changes and why
The cycle has four distinct hormonal phases, each with a different metabolic and physiological signature. The follicular phase (days 1-13, low progesterone, rising oestrogen) produces the highest anabolic capacity, best recovery, and highest pain tolerance. The luteal phase (days 15-28, high progesterone, declining oestrogen) is catabolic, thermogenically elevated, and associated with higher relative perceived exertion for the same absolute workload. The menstrual phase (days 1-5, both hormones low) is often the lowest-energy, most pain-affected phase but is not the worst phase for training — many women train well in the menstrual phase because progesterone suppression means oestrogen relatively dominates. Ovulation (day 14) represents the peak oestrogen and testosterone point — typically the highest-strength day of the cycle.
2
Established
Oestrogen and progesterone — the key performance hormones
Oestrogen has direct anabolic and neuroprotective effects. It enhances satellite cell activation (critical for muscle repair), increases bone density signalling, improves insulin sensitivity, and provides anti-inflammatory effects that support faster recovery. Progesterone, dominant in the luteal phase, is catabolic and thermogenic — it raises resting body temperature by 0.3-0.5°C, increases relative carbohydrate oxidation, elevates resting heart rate and breathing rate, reduces glycogen storage capacity, and increases fluid retention. Training in the luteal phase with the same programme as the follicular phase will feel harder — not because of weakness, but because of physiology that genuinely changes the workload.
3
Emerging evidence
Cycle-synced programming — the evidence base
The concept of structuring training intensity to match cycle phase is supported by mechanistic evidence and small RCTs, but large-scale, well-controlled outcome trials are still emerging. The clearest evidence: strength and power output are measurably higher in the follicular phase than the luteal phase in the same women (Sung et al., 2014; McNulty et al., 2020 meta-analysis). Perceived exertion is higher for the same absolute intensity in the luteal phase. Core temperature elevation in the luteal phase impairs endurance performance at high intensities. The practical prescription is clear even if the magnitude is still being quantified: schedule your heaviest, highest-intensity sessions in the follicular phase. Reduce absolute load or volume in the late luteal phase without interpreting it as fitness decline.

The follicular phase — your peak anabolic window
Days 6-13 of the cycle, after menstrual bleeding and before ovulation, represent the optimal performance window for most women. Oestrogen rises steadily, progesterone is low, pain tolerance is highest, recovery is fastest, and muscle protein synthesis response to training is at its peak.
1
Established
Oestrogen's anabolic window — why this is the time to push
Oestrogen enhances satellite cell proliferation and differentiation — the muscle stem cells responsible for hypertrophy. It reduces oxidative stress in muscle tissue during and after training. It enhances glycogen synthesis (improving fuel availability for training) and decreases muscle protein breakdown. Studies show 20-30% greater muscle protein synthesis rate in the follicular phase versus the luteal phase for identical training stimuli. This means heavy compound training, progressive overload attempts, and higher volume are most productive here. For strength-focused women, setting PR targets during the follicular phase is supported by the physiology.
2
Established
Injury risk around ovulation — the ACL caveat
The peak oestrogen surge around ovulation (days 12-14) has a specific consequence worth knowing: high oestrogen reduces ligament and tendon stiffness, temporarily increasing ACL injury risk during high-impact, change-of-direction movements. The ACL injury rate in female athletes peaks around ovulation in multiple epidemiological studies. This does not mean avoiding training — it means being deliberate about landing mechanics, deceleration technique, and not attempting maximal plyometric jumps or new movement patterns on days 12-14. Regular resistance training and proper neuromuscular conditioning partially mitigates this risk.
3
Established
Substrate utilisation: follicular phase favours carbohydrates
Oestrogen enhances glycogen synthesis and carbohydrate oxidation during exercise. In the follicular phase, the body preferentially uses carbohydrates as a fuel source, particularly during moderate-to-high intensity exercise. This means carbohydrate availability matters more in the follicular phase — higher carbohydrate intake around training sessions produces better performance and recovery in this window. Post-training carbohydrates are particularly well utilised. This is the phase to align carbohydrate-heavy meals with training days.

The luteal phase — working with progesterone dominance
Days 15-28, after ovulation, are progesterone-dominant. Resting temperature rises. Heart rate elevates. Carbohydrate storage decreases. Recovery slows. The same session will feel harder — this is not a mental weakness, it is a predictable physiological shift that most women's training programmes completely ignore.
1
Established
Thermoregulatory shift — the performance consequence
Progesterone raises the hypothalamic temperature set point by 0.3-0.5°C. This means core temperature during exercise is higher for the same workload, sweat onset is earlier, and fluid losses are greater. Heat-sensitive performance metrics (endurance capacity, perceived exertion at high intensities, time to exhaustion) are all measurably impaired in the luteal phase compared to the follicular phase. The practical adjustment: increase hydration by 400-600ml per training session in the luteal phase. For endurance training, adjust target paces based on perceived exertion rather than fixed pace targets. For strength training, the thermogenic effect is less limiting — but perceived effort will be higher for the same load.
2
Established
Progesterone and protein catabolism
Progesterone is a catabolic hormone — it increases protein breakdown relative to the follicular phase and shifts fuel metabolism away from glycogen toward fat and amino acid oxidation. This produces two practical effects: protein requirements are higher in the luteal phase (some evidence suggests 0.1-0.2g/kg higher), and carbohydrate tolerance is reduced (lower glycogen synthesis rate and capacity). The luteal phase is the time to maintain protein intake and moderate — but not dramatically cut — carbohydrates. Attempting very low carbohydrate diets in the luteal phase (when carb oxidation already shifts toward fat) can produce more severe energy and mood effects than in the follicular phase.
3
Established
PMS, food cravings, and the serotonin connection
The progesterone drop in the late luteal phase (days 24-28) produces a corresponding drop in allopregnanolone — a progesterone metabolite that is a potent GABA agonist. This GABA withdrawal produces the anxiety, irritability, and sleep disruption of PMS through the same neurological mechanism as benzodiazepine withdrawal. Carbohydrate cravings in the late luteal phase are partly driven by serotonin — carbohydrates temporarily raise serotonin via the tryptophan pathway. Magnesium deficiency amplifies PMS symptoms (magnesium is essential for GABA function). Supplementing magnesium glycinate (300-400mg) in the two weeks before menstruation has RCT evidence for reducing PMS severity and improving sleep in the luteal phase.

Phase-based training — practical prescription
You do not need to rebuild your entire programme around your cycle. The principle is simpler: schedule your hardest sessions in the follicular window, maintain quality but reduce absolute intensity in the late luteal phase, and stop interpreting the difficulty increase as fitness decline.

Phase-based nutrition — matching intake to hormonal reality
The menstrual cycle changes substrate utilisation, caloric needs, protein requirements, and carbohydrate tolerance across the month. Fixed daily macros ignore this — and produce sub-optimal results across both phases. The adjustments are not large, but they are specific.
1
Established
Caloric needs: 100-300 kcal higher in the late luteal phase
Resting metabolic rate increases by approximately 100-300 kcal per day in the late luteal phase — driven by the thermogenic effect of progesterone and the increased substrate oxidation it drives. This means pre-menstrual hunger is not a failure of willpower — it is a genuine physiological signal reflecting elevated energy expenditure. Attempting to rigidly maintain the same caloric intake as the follicular phase in the late luteal phase adds dietary stress to already-elevated physiological stress. A 100-200 kcal daily increase (from whole food sources, particularly protein and complex carbohydrates) is appropriate and evidence-supported.
2
Established
Iron — the most common female-specific deficiency
Menstrual blood loss makes iron deficiency the most common nutritional deficiency in premenopausal women. Iron deficiency impairs aerobic exercise capacity before anaemia develops — ferritin (iron storage) below 30 ng/mL produces measurable performance impairment even with normal haemoglobin. Symptoms: fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, poor recovery, increased perceived exertion. Dietary sources: red meat (haem iron, best absorbed), dark leafy greens + vitamin C (non-haem iron, enhanced by C). If symptoms are present and dietary intake is high, serum ferritin testing is worthwhile before supplementing — iron supplementation without confirmed deficiency has side effects.
3
Emerging evidence
Cycle-synced carbohydrate periodisation
Higher carbohydrate intake in the follicular phase (matching the higher glycogen synthesis capacity and carbohydrate oxidation preference) and moderate reduction in the luteal phase (matching the shift toward fat oxidation) represents a logical nutritional cycle-sync. The evidence for this as a prescribed intervention is emerging rather than established — but the mechanistic rationale is strong. In practice: increase carbohydrate intake on training days in the follicular phase (when glycogen synthesis is highest), maintain moderate carbs in the early luteal phase, and reduce slightly (with compensatory protein increase) in the late luteal phase.
💊 Magnesium glycinate: 300–400mg before sleep, days 14–28
Supports GABA tone (reduced allopregnanolone in late luteal phase), reduces PMS symptom severity, improves luteal phase sleep quality. Best-evidenced supplement specifically for luteal phase support.
🫐 Omega-3 (EPA-rich): 2–3g/day
Anti-inflammatory EPA directly reduces prostaglandin production — the primary driver of menstrual cramping. RCT evidence for reduced dysmenorrhoea. Also supports serotonin pathway function relevant to luteal mood.
🥩 Iron monitoring: ferritin testing annually
Annual serum ferritin check if training at high volume. Target >30 ng/mL for performance; >50 ng/mL is comfortable. Address via red meat and dark greens + vitamin C before supplementing.
🌙 Vitamin D: 2000–3000 IU daily
Supports oestrogen receptor sensitivity, reduces PMS severity in deficient women, and supports bone density maintenance that oestrogen normally protects. Particularly important if perimenopause is approaching.

Perimenopause — the transition window
Perimenopause typically begins in the early-to-mid 40s, though it can start earlier. It is not a sudden event — it is a 4-10 year hormonal transition during which oestrogen production becomes irregular, then progressively declines. Understanding what is happening makes the experience less bewildering and the intervention more targeted.
1
Established
What perimenopause actually does — the full biological picture
As ovarian function declines, oestrogen production becomes irregular — sometimes very high, sometimes very low, without the regular cyclical pattern of the reproductive years. The consequences of oestrogen decline are systemic and well-documented: accelerated bone density loss (3-5% per year in the first years post-menopause without intervention), shift in fat distribution from subcutaneous to visceral, declining insulin sensitivity, reduced muscle protein synthesis, sleep disruption (particularly the hot-flush disruption of slow-wave sleep), mood instability, and cognitive effects. None of these are inevitable — each has specific, evidence-based interventions. But the window between perimenopause and menopause is the most important period to invest in the protective behaviours.
2
Established
Resistance training — the most important perimenopausal investment
Resistance training is the intervention with the strongest evidence across the largest number of perimenopausal concerns simultaneously. It partially compensates for the bone density loss that oestrogen withdrawal accelerates (loading is the primary bone formation stimulus). It maintains muscle mass and metabolic rate as oestrogen's anabolic support declines. It improves insulin sensitivity (which oestrogen normally supports). It reduces visceral fat deposition. Women who enter perimenopause with high muscle mass and high bone density have substantially better health trajectories in post-menopause than those who do not — and the window to build that reserve is the reproductive years, not perimenopause itself. If you are 35-44, this is your investment window.
3
Emerging evidence
HRT — the evidence landscape has shifted
Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) underwent a period of significant over-caution following the 2002 Women's Health Initiative study, which was later found to have significant methodological issues (wrong population, wrong timing, wrong formulation). The updated evidence, particularly from body-identical HRT initiated within 10 years of menopause (the "timing hypothesis"), shows significant reduction in cardiovascular disease, bone density loss, dementia risk, and quality of life metrics. HRT is a clinical decision requiring a GP or menopause specialist assessment — not a self-managed intervention. The point here is that the previous blanket discouragements were based on flawed data, and the current evidence supports a much more nuanced conversation with a healthcare provider than was standard 10-15 years ago.
