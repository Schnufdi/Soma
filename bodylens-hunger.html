Ghrelin control — flatten the peaks before they become cravings
Ghrelin is the hunger hormone. It rises before meals, peaks at expected eating times, and — critically — can be trained, entrained, and manipulated. Understanding it turns hunger from an emergency into a manageable signal.
Ghrelin
The hunger amplifier · rises pre-meal, suppressed by eating
Ideal state
Low baseline, gentle pre-meal rises, rapid post-meal suppression
Problem state
High baseline, sharp unpredictable spikes, slow post-meal suppression
Drives it up
Caloric restriction, irregular meal timing, poor sleep, stress/cortisol, skipping breakfast
Drives it down
Consistent meal timing, protein at first meal, adequate sleep, moderate deficit vs aggressive
1
Ghrelin is entrained to expected meal times — use this
Ghrelin operates on a circadian schedule tied to your habitual meal times. If you consistently eat at 12:00 and 18:00, ghrelin will peak slightly before those times as anticipatory preparation. The mechanism works in reverse: irregular meal timing creates ghrelin peaks without the meal that suppresses them — producing hunger signals at random times, making adherence much harder. Consistent meal timing trains ghrelin to behave predictably.
2
Protein at the first meal of the day is the most powerful ghrelin suppressor
Protein has a uniquely powerful effect on ghrelin suppression — more than fat or carbohydrate at matched caloric levels. A protein-rich first meal produces a faster, deeper ghrelin suppression that lasts longer into the day. This is the mechanism behind why a high-protein breakfast (or first meal within an IF window) dramatically reduces total daily hunger compared to a carbohydrate-dominant first meal of identical calories.
3
Sleep deprivation is the fastest route to a chronically elevated ghrelin baseline
A single bad night raises ghrelin measurably. One week of sub-6h sleep raises it significantly and maintains it elevated even after sleep is restored. This is why sleep-deprived people eat more — they are genuinely hormonally hungrier, not failing at willpower. Protecting sleep is a hunger management intervention with more impact than most dietary protocols.

Leptin stability — keep the signal strong
Leptin is the satiety and metabolic rate signal produced by fat cells. Its decline during fat loss is the primary biological driver of the increasing difficulty of dieting. Managing it is the key to sustainable fat loss.
Leptin
The satiety signal · produced by fat cells in proportion to fat mass
Ideal state
Adequate levels, normal sensitivity — hunger manageable, metabolism maintained
Problem state
Low levels (from fat loss) or leptin resistance (from obesity/inflammation) — hunger uncontrolled
Drives it down
Fat loss, sleep deprivation, aggressive caloric restriction, chronic stress
Supports it
Moderate deficit, diet breaks at maintenance, adequate sleep, omega-3, reducing chronic inflammation
1
Leptin resistance — why the satiety signal stops working
Leptin resistance is the state where leptin is present but the brain stops responding to it — similar to insulin resistance. It is produced by: chronic inflammation (elevated CRP), sleep deprivation (even brief), high triglycerides (blocking leptin transport across the blood-brain barrier), and chronic caloric surplus. In leptin resistance, you can have high leptin levels and still experience insatiable hunger — the signal is there but the receptor is not responding. Reducing inflammation, improving sleep, and lowering triglycerides through dietary changes are the interventions.
2
Diet breaks restore leptin — this is why they work
Returning to maintenance calories for 1-2 weeks allows leptin to partially recover — reducing hunger and improving metabolic rate. The fat that was lost during the deficit period does not return during a maintenance break. What does return: better hormonal signalling, improved training performance, and reduced cortisol. Diet breaks are not failure — they are the management of a predictable hormonal response.

Food noise — quieting the constant food thought
Food noise is the persistent background thinking about food — what to eat next, whether you've eaten enough, cravings that intrude on concentration. It is partly hormonal (ghrelin, NPY, AgRP) and partly psychological (restriction mindset). Both are addressable.
1
The restriction-obsession loop — restriction creates food noise, not eliminates it
Caloric restriction activates hypothalamic circuits (particularly AgRP neurons) that increase food-seeking behaviour. These are not "cravings" in the psychological sense — they are survival-driven neural activations that produce intrusive food thoughts. More severe restriction = more food noise, not less. This is why aggressive dieting produces more food obsession, not more dietary control. Moderate deficit reduces this significantly compared to aggressive restriction.
2
Protein's effect on food noise — the most powerful dietary intervention
High protein intake reduces food noise through three simultaneous pathways: ghrelin suppression (less hunger signalling), increased satiety hormones (PYY, GLP-1), and reduced dopaminergic reward response to food cues. People eating 30%+ protein from calories consistently report dramatically less food noise — not through willpower but through hormonal suppression of the signals that produce it.
3
Food planning eliminates the decision fatigue that amplifies food noise
Decision fatigue — the depletion of cognitive resources from making repeated decisions — amplifies food noise significantly. When you have not planned what you will eat, your brain treats food as an unresolved task requiring processing. Pre-planned meals eliminate this cognitive load: the decision has been made, the loop is closed. This is partly why meal prep reduces overeating so reliably — not portion control alone, but elimination of open food-decision loops throughout the day.

Emotional eating — the cortisol-dopamine loop
Emotional eating is not a character flaw. It is a neurobiological response to stress — driven by cortisol, dopamine, and the reward circuitry that evolved to motivate energy-seeking in uncertain environments. Understanding the mechanism makes it addressable.
1
The mechanism — cortisol drives craving for specifically high-reward foods
Cortisol (the stress hormone) increases appetite specifically for energy-dense, high-reward foods — not for salad. The mechanism: cortisol upregulates CRF receptors in the nucleus accumbens (the brain's reward centre), making high-fat, high-sugar foods more rewarding than usual. Simultaneously, cortisol directly increases ghrelin and reduces leptin. Stress makes you biologically hungrier and makes high-calorie food specifically more appealing — this is not a choice.
2
The dopamine anticipation loop — why certain foods are "triggering"
Dopamine is released in anticipation of high-reward food, not just in response to eating it. The sight, smell, or thought of a high-reward food produces a dopamine spike that motivates approach behaviour — this is the "craving" sensation. For foods that have been repeatedly paired with eating occasions, this anticipatory dopamine release can be very strong. The solution is not more willpower — it is modifying the cue-behaviour-reward loop.
3
Interrupting the loop — the practical interventions
The cue-behaviour-reward loop can be interrupted at any point. At the cue: environmental design (don't have trigger foods at home). At the behaviour: alternative responses to the same cue (walk, drink water, call someone — the goal is to reduce the cortisol that's driving the craving, not just white-knuckle through it). At the reward: protein-forward alternatives that satisfy without derailing the programme. The most effective intervention is cortisol reduction itself — because this removes the biochemical driver of the entire loop.

Daily protocol — making hunger structural not willpower-dependent
The goal is a daily structure where hunger is predictable, manageable, and doesn't require decisions in the moment. Build the system once; run on autopilot.
Morning — set the hormonal tone for the day
First meal high protein within the eating window. This produces the most powerful all-day ghrelin suppression. Consistent timing entrains ghrelin to peak at meal times — not randomly. Coffee after cortisol peak (90min post-wake) prevents the cortisol-caffeine hunger amplification.
Protein at every meal — the satiety anchor
Leucine threshold (2.5g per meal) triggers mTOR and also maximises the satiety effect. Every meal anchored by 25-40g protein before carbohydrates and fats. This sequence — protein first, then carbs — reduces postprandial glucose spike and extends satiety.
Fibre and volume — physical fullness without calories
Gastric stretch receptors signal fullness independently of caloric content. High-volume, high-fibre foods (vegetables, legumes) activate these receptors, triggering CCK and GLP-1 release — extending satiety. The combination of protein + fibre at each meal is the most reliable appetite management strategy.
Evening — protect against the hunger peak
Hunger is typically highest in the evening — ghrelin peaks in the late afternoon/early evening by circadian design. Plan for this: a satisfying dinner within the eating window that includes protein, fibre, and adequate volume. Closing the eating window at a consistent time re-trains the ghrelin rhythm to not peak later.
Sleep — the hunger reset
Sleep is the most powerful hunger management intervention available. Adequate sleep reduces ghrelin baseline, restores leptin sensitivity, reduces cortisol, and resets the dopamine reward system. One bad night undoes multiple days of excellent dietary management at the hormonal level.
Trigger food management — environmental design
The most reliable strategy is not willpower — it is not having trigger foods accessible. A craving peaks and subsides within 15-20 minutes if not acted upon. Physical distance from the trigger is more effective than mental resistance. Design the environment, not just the mindset.
