How it's built
From answers to programme.
BodyLens doesn't use a template. It uses a reasoning model — Claude — to interpret your profile the way a senior performance coach would, then apply the relevant science to your specific situation. Here's the exact process.
1
Profile intake
The onboarding form collects 13 data points through visual selectors — age, sex, weight, body fat, goal, training history, gym access, eating patterns, trigger foods, injuries, sleep, alcohol, non-negotiables. A short AI follow-up conversation fills in what can't be captured by multiple choice — injury detail, goal nuance, lifestyle constraints. The AI is given strict instructions to ask one question at a time and probe injuries thoroughly. Your sex is a primary filter — female and male physiology produce materially different programmes.
2
Programme generation
The full profile is sent to Claude with a structured prompt that requires it to reason through macros, training split, injury modifications, supplement stack, and a coaching brief. The model is instructed to derive everything from the actual situation — not use generic targets. It produces: calorie target with reasoning, protein/carbs/fat breakdown, 7-day week plan with exercises and coach notes per day, injury-specific movement modifications, supplement stack with timing and mechanism, a coach summary, and a full instructions document.
3
Daily plan generation
Every morning, the day page reads the stored profile and generates a structured timeline for that specific day. It knows whether it's a training or rest day, the exact exercises, any injury modifications, your eating window, your food exclusions, your supplement timing relative to your wake time. The plan is cached for the day so it loads instantly on every open after the first. The ask-anything bar answers questions in full context — it knows the plan, the macros, the day type, and the food log.
4
Science personalisation
Every science page — longevity, alcohol, training, fuel, hunger, synthesis — generates a personalised commentary panel from your profile. The panel accounts for your sex, age, goal, and specific data. A 39-year-old woman reading the longevity page gets commentary on oestrogen dynamics and perimenopause proximity, not the testosterone decline narrative written for a 44-year-old man. The panels are generated once per day and cached.
5
The coach
The ask-anything bar on every page is a context-aware AI coach. It has access to your full profile, today's plan, your macro targets, and what you've logged. It answers questions about training, nutrition, recovery, and supplements in the context of who you are and what today looks like. It does not give generic advice — every response is filtered through your programme.

The maths
How your calories are calculated.
The calorie calculation uses Mifflin-St Jeor — the most validated BMR equation for most adults — then applies an activity multiplier based on training frequency, then adjusts for goal. The AI then reviews these numbers in the context of your full profile and may modify them based on factors the formula doesn't capture.
Step 1 — Basal Metabolic Rate (Mifflin-St Jeor)
Male: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Female: BMR = (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) − 161
The −161 vs +5 offset reflects the average metabolic difference between sexes at rest. This is why sex is a primary input — not a demographic label, but a physiological variable with direct numerical consequence.
Step 2 — TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)
TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier

3 training days/week → × 1.55
4 training days/week → × 1.65
5 training days/week → × 1.725
6 training days/week → × 1.80
These multipliers account for both structured training and general daily movement. The multiplier is the most variable factor — underestimating it produces chronic under-fuelling; overestimating it stalls fat loss.
Step 3 — Goal adjustment
Fat loss → TDEE − 300 to 500 kcal (moderate deficit)
Recomposition → TDEE ± 0 to 150 kcal (maintenance with protein surplus)
Muscle gain → TDEE + 150 to 250 kcal (lean surplus)
Performance → TDEE + variable (fuel the work)
Larger deficits produce faster scale movement but accelerate lean mass loss — protein becomes the primary protection mechanism. The AI coaches for the largest deficit that still preserves muscle, which is typically 300–500 kcal for most people.
Step 4 — Protein target
Advanced lifters: 2.0–2.2g per kg bodyweight
Intermediate: 1.8–2.0g per kg
Beginner: 1.6–1.8g per kg
In deficit: upper end of range (muscle preservation)
Female: same g/kg — but often lower total due to lower bodyweight
Protein target is the most important number in the programme. It is non-negotiable across all day types. Every meal must clear ~2.5g leucine to trigger mTOR activation — distribution matters as much as total.
Step 5 — Carbs and fat
Fat = max(bodyweight × 1.0g, 25% of total calories / 9)
Carbs = (Total calories − protein×4 − fat×9) / 4
Fat minimum is set at 1g/kg to protect hormonal function — particularly testosterone in men, oestrogen in women. Carbs are the residual variable — they fill the remaining calorie space after protein and essential fat are accounted for. Higher carbs on training days, lower on rest.

Split logic
Why the week is structured the way it is.
The training split is not arbitrary. Each decision — which days to train, what order to sequence muscle groups, where to place rest days — follows from both the science of recovery and your specific constraints.
📈
Progressive overload
The only mechanism for long-term strength and size gain. Each session must provide a greater stimulus than the last — more weight, more reps, less rest, or better technique. The programme is built around this as the primary principle.
⏱️
Muscle protein synthesis window
MPS remains elevated 24–48h post-session. Rest days are not wasted — they are when the adaptation happens. This is why rest day nutrition (especially protein) is as important as training day nutrition.
🔄
Recovery debt
Training too frequently on the same muscle group before full recovery produces diminishing returns and eventual overtraining. The split ensures each group gets 48–72h before its next stimulus.
🩹
Injury modification logic
Where injuries exist, the AI identifies the movement pattern to avoid, finds the closest alternative that produces the same muscular stimulus, and notes it per session. The goal is full stimulus with zero aggravation.
Why each session is placed where it is
Push sessions on Monday or Tuesday: CNS is freshest after the weekend. Bench press and overhead press are the most CNS-demanding movements — they benefit from maximum neural freshness.

Pull sessions mid-week: 48h after push allows the shoulder joint to recover (press and pull share the shoulder complex) while back muscles are independent and ready.

Posterior chain / leg sessions at week end: Glutes and hamstrings benefit from the full glycogen reload of the mid-week rest day. Lower body sessions also produce the largest acute hormonal response — for men: testosterone and GH; for women: oestrogen-driven MPS — which benefits from being followed by a rest day.

Rest days after heavy sessions: Not because training every day is harmful, but because adaptation requires the anabolic window to complete without interference.

Food as signal
Why food choices are structured the way they are.
Every food recommendation is derived from two questions: does it hit the leucine threshold to trigger mTOR? And does it support the goal without creating the conditions for overeating? The programme is built around protein anchors — foods that clear the 2.5g leucine threshold cleanly — with carbs and fat built around them.
The leucine threshold
Why each meal needs ~30g protein.
mTOR — the molecular switch for muscle protein synthesis — is activated by leucine, an amino acid in protein. The threshold is approximately 2.5g leucine per meal, which corresponds to roughly 25–35g complete protein depending on the source. Below this threshold, MPS is sub-maximally stimulated regardless of total daily protein.

This is why protein distribution across meals matters — not just the daily total. Four meals of 40g protein produces more total MPS than one meal of 160g and three of 0g.
Eating window logic
Why the programme respects your fasting window.
If your eating window is compressed (e.g. 12:00–20:00), the programme must fit your full protein target into fewer meals — which means higher per-meal protein targets and fewer opportunities for glycaemic crashes. The benefit: insulin sensitivity is elevated after the fasting period, which improves nutrient partitioning when you break the fast. The risk: if meals are too small, you may miss the leucine threshold. The programme balances these by setting meal sizes that clear the threshold within your window.
Trigger food strategy
Why the programme names specific foods to manage.
Trigger foods are not moral failures — they are foods that, once in the environment, produce consumption patterns that break the calorie target. The programme names them explicitly and provides a management strategy: environmental control (don't keep them in the house), substitution (have a high-protein alternative ready), and planned inclusion (if alcohol or snacks are a trigger, schedule them on rest days with a defined limit rather than leaving them unplanned).
Sex-specific nutrition notes
♀️
Female nutrition considerations
Oestrogen affects nutrient partitioning — women tend to oxidise more fat and less carbohydrate than men at the same relative exercise intensity. In the luteal phase (post-ovulation), resting metabolic rate increases ~100–300 kcal and cravings intensify. Higher iron requirements (1.8× men). Same protein g/kg target, typically lower total calories.
♂️
Male nutrition considerations
Higher absolute calorie requirements. Greater carbohydrate oxidation during training. Testosterone supports higher MPS rates, which means slightly higher protein synthesis ceiling. More susceptible to alcohol-induced testosterone suppression — the programme accounts for this in the alcohol protocol.
Why supplements are in the stack
The mechanism behind each one.
Every supplement in the stack is selected because it addresses a specific gap that food alone cannot close, or because the evidence for its mechanism is strong enough to justify inclusion. Nothing is in the stack for branding reasons. The timing for each is specific — not morning vs night, but relative to training, cortisol windows, and sleep architecture.
Cr
Creatine monohydrate
Mechanism: Phosphocreatine resynthesis — replenishes ATP during high-intensity efforts, allowing more reps at the same weight over time. Effect size: 5–15% strength increase across hundreds of RCTs. Why it's timed any time: daily saturation matters more than acute timing. Loading is unnecessary — 5g daily reaches saturation within 3–4 weeks.
ω3
Omega-3 EPA/DHA
Mechanism: COX pathway suppression — reduces systemic and joint inflammation by competing with arachidonic acid for the same enzymes. Also directly amplifies the MPS response to protein. Why it's timed with food: fat-soluble, requires dietary fat for absorption. Higher doses are better — 2–3g EPA+DHA not total omega-3.
D3
Vitamin D3 + K2
Mechanism: Vitamin D3 drives testosterone production via the hypothalamic-pituitary axis and immune function. Near-universal deficiency in UK/northern Europe Oct–Apr. K2 directs calcium into bone rather than arteries. Why they're paired: D3 increases calcium absorption; K2 ensures it goes to bone. Separate them and you get incomplete benefit.
Mg
Magnesium glycinate
Mechanism: GABA receptor cofactor — enables the GABAergic inhibition that allows the brain to switch from alert to sleep mode. Why glycinate specifically: most bioavailable form, doesn't cause the GI distress of magnesium oxide or citrate at sleep doses. Why before sleep: the GH pulse happens in the first slow-wave cycle. Anything that fragments or delays sleep reduces it.
Col
Collagen peptides + Vitamin C
Mechanism: Shaw et al. 2017 — taking collagen peptides with Vitamin C 45–60 minutes before training produces 5.5× the connective tissue synthesis during the loading window. The mechanical loading of training stimulates collagen cross-linking; the supplemental collagen provides the substrate; the Vitamin C enables hydroxylation (the chemistry that makes collagen structurally functional). Directly relevant to any tendon, ligament, or joint injury.
L-T
L-Theanine
Mechanism: Alpha wave modulation — increases alpha wave activity in the brain, producing relaxed alertness. Taken with caffeine, it removes the anxiety and jitteriness while preserving the focus and alertness. Why it's paired with coffee: caffeine alone produces cortisol elevation and HPA axis activation that degrades the quality of the focus state. L-Theanine neutralises this.
Ash
Ashwagandha KSM-66
Mechanism: HPA axis modulation — reduces cortisol 25–30% in RCTs (Chandrasekhar 2012), and supports testosterone via the HPT axis. Why KSM-66 specifically: the most studied extract with the highest withanolide concentration. Most ashwagandha products use inferior extracts. Particularly relevant for high-stress periods, sleep disruption, and anyone over 35 experiencing hormonal decline.
