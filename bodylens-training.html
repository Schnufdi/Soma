Frequency — how often per muscle group
The most argued variable in training. The answer from the research is clearer than most people realise — but it comes with important caveats about volume, recovery, and individual variation.
1
Muscle protein synthesis elevates for 48–72 hours after a session — then returns to baseline
This single physiological fact defines why frequency matters. After a training session, MPS is elevated for roughly 48-72 hours — then returns to baseline regardless of whether you train again or not. Once-weekly training per muscle means approximately 4 out of every 7 days the muscle is not being stimulated to grow. Twice-weekly training keeps the stimulus more continuous. Daily training on the same muscle (without adequate recovery time) loads before the previous session's repair is complete — becoming counterproductive.
2
Schoenfeld et al. (2016) — twice weekly produces ~70% more hypertrophy than once weekly at matched volume
This is the landmark meta-analysis that settled the frequency debate for hypertrophy. When volume is equalised, twice-weekly frequency wins. The mechanism: two MPS elevation windows per week versus one, with no additional fatigue cost when volume is matched. Twice weekly = ~70% more hypertrophy than once weekly at matched volume.
3
Daily training of the same muscle is counterproductive — not because of soreness but because of incomplete repair
DOMS is not the limiting factor — myofibrillar repair is. After an intense session, the structural proteins in muscle fibres are being remodelled. Loading the same fibres before this repair completes impairs the remodelling process. The exception: high-frequency, low-volume approaches (Bulgarian method) for strength skill acquisition — where the goal is neural efficiency, not hypertrophy. Daily training valid for strength skill — not for muscle building.

Training splits — every major approach
A training split determines which muscles you train on which days. Each has different frequency, recovery, and volume characteristics. The right split depends on your goal, training days, and recovery capacity.
Full body (3× per week)
High frequency
Every session trains every major muscle group with 1-2 exercises each. Best frequency per muscle — 3× per week. Low volume per session. Excellent for beginners and when training days are limited.
Best for: Beginners · Time-limited trainees · Deload weeks · Strength skill development
Upper / Lower (4× per week)
Best evidence base
Upper body twice, lower body twice. Each muscle trained 2× per week. Moderate volume per session. The most research-supported split for simultaneous strength and hypertrophy. Flexible recovery between sessions.
Best for: Intermediate to advanced · Hypertrophy + strength simultaneously
Push / Pull / Legs (PPL)
3 or 6 days
Push (chest, shoulders, triceps), Pull (back, biceps), Legs. Run 3× per week for once-weekly frequency, 6× for twice-weekly. At 6 days it becomes one of the best hypertrophy splits available. At 3 days, frequency is too low.
Best for: 5-6 day trainees · Advanced hypertrophy · High volume tolerance
Bro split (1 muscle per day)
Suboptimal frequency
Chest Monday, Back Tuesday, Shoulders Wednesday, Arms Thursday, Legs Friday. Each muscle trained once per week. Allows very high session volume but wastes 5 out of 7 MPS windows. Widely used — not widely supported by research for hypertrophy.
Better than nothing · High volume per session · Not recommended for primary hypertrophy goal

Volume & intensity — how much, how hard
Volume (sets × reps × load) and intensity (proximity to failure) are the two most critical training variables after frequency. Both have clear research-backed ranges — and a common tendency to do too much of both, or too little.
1
Weekly volume — sets per muscle group
The research consensus: 10-20 sets per muscle per week is the hypertrophy range for most people. Below 5 sets is maintenance. Above 20 sets produces diminishing returns relative to fatigue for most intermediate lifters — though advanced athletes with high recovery capacity can productively exceed this.

Minimum effective: 5–10 sets/wk · Maintenance
Recommended: 10–20 sets/wk · Hypertrophy range
Junk volume territory: 20–30 sets/wk · Diminishing returns, fatigue accumulates
30+ sets/wk · Most people cannot recover adequately — injury risk rises
2
Proximity to failure — RPE 7–9 is the evidence-based sweet spot
Training to complete failure (RPE 10) produces no additional hypertrophy compared to stopping 1-3 reps short — and significantly increases injury risk and recovery time. Stopping too far from failure (RPE 5-6) reduces the mechanical tension that drives hypertrophy. RPE 7-9 (1-3 reps remaining) produces optimal hypertrophy stimulus with manageable fatigue. The last 5 reps of any set are the reps that drive adaptation — the preceding reps are preparation.
3
Rep ranges — the range is wider than most people think
Research (Schoenfeld 2017, Morton 2016) consistently shows equivalent hypertrophy across rep ranges from 5 to 30 reps — when sets are taken to similar proximity to failure. Low reps (5-8) build more strength alongside hypertrophy. High reps (15-30) produce more metabolic stress and less joint load — useful for injury-prone individuals or accessory work. Varying rep ranges across the week produces superior outcomes to staying in one range.

The cardio debate — interference effect, the honest answer
Does cardio kill gains? The scientific answer is more nuanced than either camp admits — and the practical answer for most people is straightforward.
✓
The case for cardio alongside resistance training
VO₂max is the strongest predictor of all-cause longevity — stronger than smoking status, blood pressure, or metabolic markers. Zone 2 cardio (3-4 hours/week) increases mitochondrial density in muscle cells, directly improving recovery between sets and between sessions. A more aerobically efficient athlete recovers faster and can handle more training volume. Cardiovascular training improves insulin sensitivity — enhancing nutrient partitioning, so more of the caloric surplus goes to muscle rather than fat.
✗
The interference effect — when cardio does impair gains
AMPK activation from aerobic exercise directly suppresses mTOR — the molecular switch for muscle building. They are competing signals. This is real. But the interference effect is clinically significant only at high cardio volumes: 4+ sessions per week, 45+ minutes each, at moderate-to-high intensity. 2-3 Zone 2 sessions of 30-45 minutes do not meaningfully impair hypertrophy in the research. The interference effect is real — but the threshold is higher than most fear.
!
The practical rule — separate cardio from resistance training
If doing both on the same day, do resistance training first — then cardio. The AMPK signal from cardio performed before resistance training suppresses the mTOR response to training. Performed after, the interference is minimal. Better still: separate them by 6+ hours or place cardio on different days entirely. Zone 2 (conversation pace, 60-70% max HR) has the smallest interference effect and the largest longevity and mitochondrial benefit.

Periodisation — how training evolves over time
Periodisation is the systematic variation of training variables over time to prevent stagnation, manage fatigue, and maximise long-term adaptation. It is not optional — it is what separates people who keep progressing from people who plateau.
1
Linear periodisation — add weight each session
Simple, effective for beginners. Works until the nervous system and muscle architecture can no longer adapt session-to-session — typically within 3-6 months for upper body and 6-12 months for lower body. At that point, weekly or block-level progression becomes necessary.
2
Block periodisation — accumulation, intensification, deload
Training divided into 3-6 week blocks with specific emphasis:

Accumulation (weeks 1-2): Volume builds, moderate intensity. The training debt accumulates.
Intensification (week 3): Volume holds, load increases, rep range drops. The stimulus peaks.
Deload (week 4): Volume reduces 40%, load maintains. The deload is not rest — it is when fatigue clears and the fitness from the previous three weeks is expressed. Skip the deload and you never collect the dividend.
3
Daily undulating periodisation (DUP) — the most research-supported advanced approach
Rather than varying training emphasis by block (weeks), DUP varies it by session (days). Monday: 3-5 reps (strength). Wednesday: 8-12 reps (hypertrophy). Friday: 15-20 reps (metabolic). Multiple meta-analyses show DUP produces superior strength and hypertrophy outcomes compared to linear approaches for intermediate-advanced lifters — because it stimulates different motor unit populations and energy systems across the week without requiring separate mesocycles.

Advanced techniques — what works, what doesn't, when to use them
Techniques beyond straight sets. All have evidence behind them. None are necessary for beginners. All have optimal use cases — and common misuse cases.
⟳
Supersets (antagonist pairing) — use on every applicable session
Pairing opposing muscle groups back-to-back with minimal rest — bench press + row, bicep curl + tricep extension. Research shows no meaningful interference between antagonist muscles: the bench press does not fatigue the muscles used in the row. Result: you accomplish the same volume in 30-40% less time. Antagonist supersets are the highest time-efficiency technique available with no downside.
↓
Drop sets — use selectively, final set of isolation exercises only
Immediately reduce load 20-30% at failure and continue for additional reps. High metabolic stress, high fatigue. Evidence: drop sets on the final set produce equivalent hypertrophy to 3 additional straight sets in significantly less time. Best use: final set of cable laterals, curls, or leg extensions — isolation exercises where fatigue is local and systemic fatigue is minimal. Avoid on compound movements where technique degrades under fatigue.
◈
Rest-pause sets — advanced stages only
Perform a set to near-failure, rest 10-15 seconds, continue for additional reps with the same load, repeat 2-3 times. Research shows rest-pause produces similar or superior hypertrophy to straight sets with 30-40% less total time under load. High CNS demand — best reserved for experienced lifters in the intensification phase of a mesocycle.
○
Blood flow restriction (BFR) — useful for injury rehab and high-frequency low-load work
Partial venous occlusion using a cuff or band at light loads (20-30% 1RM) produces hypertrophy comparable to heavy training by creating metabolic stress and cellular swelling. Evidence is strong. Primary use case: maintaining or building muscle during injury rehabilitation when heavy loading is contraindicated. Also valuable for adding high-frequency volume without CNS load.
Age & training — what actually changes and what doesn't
The fitness industry gets this wrong in both directions — either claiming age makes no difference (wishful thinking) or that it makes training futile (equally wrong). The research is clear on both what changes and what doesn't.
No meaningful difference after 40
✓
Hypertrophy response to training stimulus
Muscle tissue responds to mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and muscle damage the same way at 44 as at 24. Multiple studies directly comparing 20-35 year olds with 40-60 year olds show no significant difference in hypertrophy response when volume and intensity are matched. The adaptations are identical. What changes is the recovery time — not the growth response.
✓
Progressive overload mechanism
Adding load and volume over time produces the same adaptation curve regardless of age. The rate of absolute strength increase may be slightly slower in the first few months, but the mechanism and its response to progressive overload is unchanged. You can get significantly stronger and more muscular at 40-60 — the research is unambiguous on this.
Genuinely different after 40
!
Recovery time — the real change
Recovery capacity does decline with age, primarily due to reduced testosterone, GH, and IGF-1 output. The practical impact: for heavy compound movements, 72 hours recovery between sessions for the same muscle group produces better outcomes than 48 hours. This is already built into your split. The right response to slower recovery is smart programming — not less training.
!
Injury risk and joint tolerance
Connective tissue — tendons, ligaments, cartilage — repairs more slowly with age. The training stimulus that was fine at 25 may produce overuse injuries at 45 if recovery is inadequate. Collagen supplementation (10-15g collagen + vitamin C, 45 minutes before training) has strong evidence for tendon and connective tissue support. Managing training load and not skipping deloads becomes more important, not less.
!
Hormonal environment
Testosterone declines ~1-2% annually from age 30 in men. This reduces the anabolic environment — but does not eliminate it. The training stimulus for hypertrophy still works; the hormonal environment is simply less amplifying. This is why the programme places extra emphasis on sleep quality (the primary GH window), protein sufficiency (compensating for mild anabolic resistance), and the supplement stack (zinc, magnesium, ashwagandha — all supporting the hormonal environment naturally).
