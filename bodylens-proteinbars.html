Protein bars:
the honest read.

They're not evil. They're not magic. Most are a reasonable compromise in specific situations — and a poor replacement for food in most others. Here's exactly when they earn their place and when they don't.

⚖️
The verdict: useful tool, not a food group
A good protein bar covers a genuine gap — post-training when a meal is delayed, travel, or a day where protein is running short. A bad one is a confectionery product with a health halo. The difference is in the label, not the marketing.
The protein quality problem

The number on the front — "20g protein" — doesn't tell you what kind of protein. Most bars use a blend of whey concentrate, collagen, and soy isolate. Whey concentrate is solid. The others are cheaper, absorbed less efficiently, and have a weaker leucine signal — the amino acid that actually triggers muscle protein synthesis.

Why this matters
Leucine is the trigger, not the total
MPS (muscle protein synthesis) is triggered by a leucine threshold of around 2–3g per meal. Whey protein hits this easily. Collagen protein is essentially leucine-deficient — it counts toward your gram total but contributes almost nothing to the anabolic signal. Some bars hit 20g total but only 14g of that is high-quality protein. You're paying a premium for numbers, not signal.
Read the ingredient list. If collagen is in the top 3 protein sources, discount the protein count by 20–30%.
The sugar alcohol issue
Maltitol
Has a glycaemic index of 35 — not zero. Spikes insulin more than the label implies. Causes significant GI distress in doses above 10g, which most bars hit. Widespread in Quest-style bars. Listed on labels as "sugar alcohol" so it's excluded from the sugar count — misleading.
Erythritol + stevia blend
Far better than maltitol. Erythritol passes through mostly unabsorbed, minimal GI effect. Stevia is calorie-free and doesn't spike insulin. This is the sweetener combination you want.
Glycerin (glycerol)
Counted as a carb but has minimal insulin impact. Used to keep bars chewy. Not a red flag at low doses but inflates the carb total — factor this in if you're carb-counting.
Coating chocolate with palm oil
Most "chocolate-flavoured" coatings use palm kernel oil rather than cocoa butter. High in saturated fat, low in anything useful. Adds 5–8g fat with zero nutritional benefit. The bar suddenly has 18g fat — almost a third of a rest-day fat budget.
Inulin / chicory root fibre
Actually useful. Prebiotic fibre that feeds gut bacteria, improves satiety, and doesn't spike blood sugar. Bars that use it to hit their fibre numbers are doing the right thing. Some people experience mild bloating — a 5–10 day adaptation window normally resolves it.
When they actually earn their place
✓ Good use cases
Post-training, no meal ready — better than waiting 2+ hours. Protein window still matters.
Travel or airports — airport food is uniformly terrible for protein. A bar bridges the gap.
Protein running short at 9pm — if you're 30g under target and the kitchen is closed, a bar is the right call.
Pre-training if training fasted — 20g protein + a little carb, 60 min before, without a heavy stomach.
✗ Poor use cases
Replacing a meal — 230 kcal doesn't constitute a meal. You'll be under on calories and still hungry.
Daily habit without tracking — they're easy to over-consume. The "healthy snack" that adds 500 kcal to a rest day.
Instead of protein-dense real food — Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, or eggs beat every bar on cost, nutrient density, and satiety.
Bedtime protein — whey digests fast. Casein (cottage cheese, Greek yoghurt) is far superior for overnight MPS.

Your context
The rule
The three-question test
Before you buy a bar, ask three things
1. Is there real food available? If yes, eat that. Greek yoghurt, eggs, chicken, tuna, cottage cheese — all beat every bar on satiety, cost, and nutrient density.

2. Does it have 20g+ protein from whey or milk protein (not collagen)? If the bar doesn't clear this threshold, it's not a protein bar — it's a snack bar with marketing.

3. Is the sweetener erythritol, stevia, or nothing? Maltitol is a dealbreaker if you're training the same day. The GI distress risk is real.
If all three are satisfied: fine to use as a tactical tool. If one fails: pick a different bar or eat real food.
