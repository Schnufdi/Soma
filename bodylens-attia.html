Dr Peter Attia
MD · Stanford, Johns Hopkins, NIH · Author of Outlive · The Drive podcast
Physician-researcher
Attia trained as a surgeon and spent years at the NIH studying cancer metabolism before pivoting to longevity medicine. His framework centres on what he calls the "Four Horsemen" — cardiovascular disease, cancer, neurodegenerative disease, and metabolic dysfunction — and the interventions that most powerfully delay each. His protocols are heavily weighted toward peer-reviewed evidence and his own clinical experience with patients.
Cardiorespiratory fitness
Zone 2 training — the longevity lever
Strong evidence
VO₂max is the single strongest predictor of all-cause mortality that exists — stronger than smoking status, stronger than any biomarker. Each 1 MET increase in cardiorespiratory fitness reduces all-cause mortality risk by approximately 13%. Moving from the bottom quartile to above-average VO₂max reduces mortality risk by 45%.

Zone 2 — the highest intensity at which you can still hold a conversation and stay in fat oxidation — is the zone that builds mitochondrial density and metabolic flexibility. Attia recommends 3-4 hours per week for people serious about longevity.
The protocol: 45-60 min continuous aerobic work at 60-70% max heart rate — the pace where you can speak in full sentences but would rather not. Rowing, cycling, swimming, brisk walking uphill. 3-4 sessions per week for longevity; at least 150 min for baseline health.
Source: Kokkinos et al., JACC 2022 · Attia, Outlive (2023), Ch.11 · peterattiamd.com/zone2
VO₂max — your most important fitness number
Strong evidence
VO₂max declines ~10% per decade from age 30 in sedentary people, and ~5% per decade in trained individuals. Because the decline is exponential and threshold-dependent — falling below certain levels triggers massive mortality risk step-changes — maintaining VO₂max across decades is one of the highest-leverage interventions available.
The protocol: Once weekly VO₂max-targeted interval session — 4×4 min at 90-95% max HR with 4 min active recovery. This is the most efficient way to raise the ceiling. Zone 2 work maintains and builds the base; intervals push the ceiling up.
Source: Helgerud et al., Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise 2007 · peterattiamd.com/vo2max
Strength & muscle
Muscle mass as longevity currency
Strong evidence
Muscle mass and grip strength are independently predictive of all-cause mortality, independent of body fat percentage. Sarcopenia — age-related muscle loss — accelerates after 40 at roughly 3-5% per decade without intervention. Each decade of inaction compounds the loss. The earlier you build a "muscle reserve," the more buffer you have against the inevitable decline.
The protocol: Progressive resistance training 3-4x per week. Prioritise compound movements. Track loads and add weight progressively — the progressive overload principle is non-negotiable. Attia specifically emphasises eccentric strength, single-leg stability, and grip strength as the metrics that best predict functional independence at 80.
Source: Ruiz et al., BMJ 2008 · Newman et al., Journals of Gerontology 2006 · peterattiamd.com/exercise
Biomarkers — know your numbers
Attia's central argument: most people discover cardiovascular disease when they have a heart attack. By then, the atherosclerotic process has been running for decades. Getting ahead of it requires measuring markers that predict risk 20-30 years out — not markers that confirm disease that's already there.

Talk to your GP — what to ask for
Actionable
Most standard NHS or GP blood panels do not include ApoB or Lp(a) — they use LDL-C which is a less accurate risk predictor. You can request expanded lipid panels, and private testing is affordable (Medichecks, Thriva, Check4Cancer all offer relevant panels in the UK).
Ask your GP for: Full lipid panel including ApoB · Lp(a) (once in your life) · HbA1c · Fasting glucose + insulin · Liver function (including ALT specifically) · Ferritin · Vitamin D · Thyroid (TSH, free T3, free T4)
UK private testing: medichecks.com · thriva.co

Dr Andrew Huberman
PhD Neuroscience · Stanford School of Medicine · Huberman Lab podcast
Neuroscientist
Huberman is a research neuroscientist at Stanford whose lab studies the visual system and brain-body connections. He translates neuroscience and adjacent research for a general audience. His protocols vary in evidence quality — some are strongly supported (morning light exposure, NSDR), others are extrapolated from animal models or early-phase research. We note the evidence level for each.
Note on evidence quality: Huberman draws from a wide range of research. Where protocols rest primarily on animal studies, small sample sizes, or single trials, we flag this. The science is real — the translation to human application varies in certainty.
Morning & circadian biology
Morning sunlight — the master circadian signal
Strong evidence
Light exposure in the first hour after waking is the primary zeitgeber — the signal that sets your circadian clock. Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) signal the suprachiasmatic nucleus directly. This sets the timing of cortisol peak, melatonin onset 14-16 hours later, and core body temperature rhythm. The effects cascade through metabolism, sleep architecture, mood regulation, and hormonal timing.
The protocol: 5-10 minutes of outdoor light within 30-60 minutes of waking. On cloudy days, 15-20 minutes. No sunglasses (the signal needs to reach ipRGCs). Do not look directly at the sun. Inside windows block the short-wavelength light that drives the signal most effectively.
Source: Czeisler & Gooley, Science 2007 · Brainard et al., J Neuroscience 2001 · hubermanlab.com
Delay caffeine 90-120 minutes post-wake
Moderate evidence
Cortisol peaks 30-45 minutes after waking and naturally clears adenosine (the sleep pressure molecule that caffeine blocks). Consuming caffeine during this window adds nothing — cortisol is already performing the alerting function. Consuming caffeine after the cortisol peak uses adenosine blockade when you actually need it, extending alertness and reducing the afternoon energy crash.
The protocol: Water and morning light first. Coffee or tea 90-120 minutes after waking. This is built into your BodyLens daily schedule already.
Source: Lovallo et al., Pharmacology Biochemistry and Behavior 2005 · Based on cortisol awakening response research — the specific 90-min delay is Huberman's extrapolation from the underlying physiology.
Sleep architecture
NSDR — Non-Sleep Deep Rest
Moderate evidence
NSDR (also: Yoga Nidra) is a deliberate practice of inducing a state between waking and sleeping — low-alpha, high-delta brainwaves — through guided body scanning and breath control. Huberman's research and adjacent yoga nidra studies show measurable increases in dopamine release in the striatum (up to 65% in one small study), restoration of cognitive performance, and a learning-consolidation effect. The evidence for learning enhancement is particularly interesting: NSDR performed after a learning session appears to accelerate consolidation.
The protocol: 10-20 minutes of guided NSDR or Yoga Nidra — Huberman's free version on YouTube, or any Yoga Nidra recording. Ideally after training or a cognitively demanding period. Not a substitute for sleep — a complement to it.
Source: Kjaer et al., Cognitive Brain Research 2002 (yoga nidra + dopamine) · Huberman NSDR (YouTube)
Cold & heat protocols
Cold exposure — dopamine, noradrenaline, and recovery
Moderate evidence
Cold water immersion (10-15°C for 2-3 minutes) produces a significant catecholamine surge — noradrenaline and dopamine — with a lasting effect that extends well beyond the cold exposure itself. Huberman cites studies showing 200-300% increases in noradrenaline and longer-duration dopamine elevation compared to other stimuli. The mood and focus effects are well-documented. The recovery effect (inflammation reduction) is real but may be counterproductive immediately post-training — it can blunt the training adaptation signal.
The protocol: Cold shower or cold plunge 10-15°C for 2-3 minutes. Not immediately post-training if your goal is hypertrophy — the inflammatory response is part of the adaptation. Best timed 4+ hours after resistance training or on rest days. Morning timing aligns with the catecholamine effect most usefully.
Source: Søberg et al., Cell Reports Medicine 2021 · Tipton et al., J Physiology 2017 (re: post-training cold blunting adaptation)
Sauna — cardiovascular and longevity effects
Strong evidence
The Finnish sauna studies (Laukkanen et al.) are among the most compelling data in longevity research. In a 20-year cohort study of 2,315 men, those using sauna 4-7 times per week had 40% lower all-cause mortality and 50% lower cardiovascular mortality compared to once-per-week users. The mechanisms include: heat shock protein induction, plasma volume expansion (mimicking cardiac training effect), and growth hormone release.
The protocol: 80-100°C for 20 minutes, 3-4 times per week minimum to see the cardiovascular benefit. Huberman and Attia both recommend this. Hydrate well before and after — 500ml minimum.
Source: Laukkanen et al., JAMA Internal Medicine 2015 · JAMA Internal Medicine 2018 · peterattiamd.com/sauna
Supplements — what both agree on
Both Attia and Huberman are cautious about supplements relative to foundational behaviours. Attia's view: most supplements are noise; the signal comes from sleep, exercise, and diet. Huberman is more open to specific neurological protocols. Where both agree, the evidence is worth taking seriously.
What both recommend
Strong evidence
Creatine monohydrate 3-5g/day — the most well-evidenced performance supplement. Increases PCr availability, improves strength output, and has emerging cognitive benefits. Both consider it a baseline for anyone who trains.
Vitamin D3 with K2 — most people in Northern Europe are deficient. Attia considers it among the highest-ROI supplements. Aim for serum 25(OH)D of 40-60 ng/mL. Test first.
Magnesium glycinate or threonate 300-400mg — ubiquitous deficiency in Western diets, especially in athletes. Improved sleep quality is well-documented. Attia uses magnesium threonate for cognitive effects; Huberman recommends glycinate for sleep.
Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) 2-3g/day — anti-inflammatory, cardiovascular protective, potentially mood-supportive. Both prioritise EPA for mood. Attia specifically targets an omega-3 index of >8%.
