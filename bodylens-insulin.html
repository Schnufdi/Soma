⚠ Important — who this is not for
BodyLens is not designed for people with diabetes or medically managed glucose conditions.
The meal timing, fasting windows, and macronutrient targets in this programme are built for metabolically healthy adults. If you take insulin, sulphonylureas, or any glucose-lowering medication, following these recommendations without medical supervision could cause a hypoglycaemic event. This is not a disclaimer — it is a hard clinical contraindication.
  • Type 1 diabetes — insulin-dependent. BodyLens does not account for exogenous insulin dosing.
  • Type 2 diabetes managed with insulin or sulphonylureas — fasting windows and low-carb meal timing can cause dangerous hypoglycaemia without medication adjustment.
  • Type 2 managed with metformin only — lower risk, but please discuss any fasting protocol with your GP before starting.
  • Reactive hypoglycaemia — the structured meal windows may not be appropriate without medical input.
  • Gestational diabetes — specific nutritional requirements differ from this programme.
What insulin actually is

Insulin is not the enemy. It is a signalling hormone produced by the pancreatic beta cells in response to rising blood glucose. Its job is to open the door — to allow glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids to enter cells. Without insulin, nutrients circulate in the blood and cannot be used.

The problem is not insulin itself. The problem is chronically elevated insulin — a state that emerges from constant eating, very high carbohydrate intake, poor sleep, and chronic stress. Chronically elevated insulin keeps the body in a perpetual storage mode: fat burning is suppressed, fat cells are preferentially filled, and over time, cells become resistant to insulin's signal.

The two modes
01
Fed state — insulin elevated
Glucose and amino acids are being absorbed. Insulin is high. The body is in an anabolic, storage-first state. Muscle protein synthesis is active (if amino acids are available). Fat oxidation is suppressed — you are not burning fat while insulin is elevated. This is not a problem. It is the correct response to eating.
02
Fasted state — insulin low
3–5 hours after a meal, insulin has cleared. Glucagon rises. The body begins mobilising stored energy — first glycogen, then fat. This is the only metabolic state in which significant fat oxidation occurs. Intermittent fasting works by extending this window. The fasting window in your programme is not arbitrary — it is designed to maximise daily time with insulin suppressed.
03
Insulin resistance — the slow accumulation
When cells are repeatedly exposed to high insulin, they downregulate their insulin receptors. The pancreas compensates by producing more insulin. This cycle — higher insulin required to achieve the same effect — is insulin resistance. It develops over years. Early insulin resistance dramatically impairs body composition: nutrients are preferentially stored as fat rather than directed to muscle. The single most effective lifestyle interventions for insulin sensitivity are resistance training, sleep, and reducing meal frequency.
What happens after you eat

Every meal produces a glucose curve — a rise, a peak, and a return to baseline. The shape of that curve determines how much insulin is secreted, how long fat oxidation is suppressed, and how hungry you will be 2–3 hours later.

The area under the curve represents total insulin secretion. A flatter, slower curve means less total insulin, longer time in fat-burning mode, and more stable energy and hunger for the next 3 hours.

What shapes the curve
Glycaemic index vs glycaemic load
GI measures how fast a food raises blood glucose. GL measures GI multiplied by the amount of carbohydrate. Watermelon has a high GI but low GL — a small serving raises glucose modestly. White rice has a high GI and high GL. Practical rule: GL matters more than GI. Portion size and food combinations determine actual glucose response.
Food ordering
Eating protein and vegetables before carbohydrates reduces peak glucose by 30–40% compared to eating carbohydrates first. The fibre and protein slow gastric emptying and reduce the rate of glucose absorption. This is not a minor effect — it is one of the most impactful zero-cost interventions available.
Fibre
Soluble fibre forms a viscous gel in the gut that slows glucose absorption. It also feeds gut bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids, which improve insulin sensitivity directly. Target: 30g fibre per day minimum. Most people consume 12–15g.
Exercise timing
A 10–15 minute walk after a meal lowers peak glucose by 20–30%. Working muscle acts as a glucose sink independent of insulin — GLUT4 transporters move to the cell surface without insulin signalling. This is why post-meal movement is one of the most effective glucose management tools available.
01
The reactive hypoglycaemia trap
A steep glucose spike followed by rapid insulin secretion often overshoots — glucose drops below the fasted baseline 2–3 hours after eating. This sub-baseline dip drives intense hunger, cravings, and poor food choices. Most "I'm hungry again an hour after eating" experiences are reactive hypoglycaemia — caused by the previous meal's composition, not actual energy shortage.
02
The dawn phenomenon
Cortisol and growth hormone rise in the early morning hours (4–8am) and trigger a natural glucose release — the body preparing for waking activity. This is normal physiology. For most people, fasted morning training uses this natural glucose availability. Eating immediately on waking suppresses this window and requires insulin before the cortisol peak has cleared.
Why insulin management is central to recomposition

Body recomposition — losing fat while building or maintaining muscle — requires the body to be in two different states at different times. Insulin is the switch that determines which state you're in.

01
Nutrient partitioning: where does the food actually go?
When you eat, insulin determines whether the incoming nutrients are directed to muscle cells or fat cells. Good insulin sensitivity means a higher proportion goes to muscle. Poor insulin sensitivity means fat cells capture a greater share — even when calories are appropriate. This is why two people eating identical calories can have very different body composition outcomes. Improving insulin sensitivity is one of the highest-leverage recomposition interventions.
02
The anabolic window and post-training insulin
Resistance training depletes muscle glycogen and activates mTOR. In this window, muscle cells are maximally insulin sensitive — they will absorb glucose and amino acids preferentially. This is the one time when a moderate insulin spike is actively beneficial. Fast carbohydrates + fast protein within 30–45 minutes post-training uses the elevated mTOR and insulin sensitivity to drive muscle protein synthesis and glycogen resynthesis simultaneously.
03
Fasting window: the fat-burning phase
During the fasted window — typically 12–16 hours overnight — insulin is suppressed, glucagon is elevated, and the body is in net fat-oxidation mode. This window is not about starvation; it is about allowing insulin to clear completely so fat stores can be accessed. Eating late at night or immediately on waking shortens this window and reduces total daily time in fat-burning mode, regardless of what the calories look like.
04
Insulin and cortisol: the double hit
Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which raises blood glucose, which triggers insulin. The combination of high cortisol and high insulin is particularly damaging to body composition: cortisol promotes visceral fat accumulation (abdominal fat cells have dense cortisol receptors) while simultaneously breaking down muscle tissue. Stress management is not a soft metric — it directly affects the insulin environment your programme operates in.
The recomposition equation
Recomposition requires controlled insulin pulses timed around training, a sustained fasting window for fat oxidation, and improved baseline insulin sensitivity from resistance training and sleep. Your programme is structured to do all three simultaneously. The meal timing is not arbitrary preference — it is insulin management applied to your training schedule.
Evidence-based glucose management for healthy people

These are the highest-impact, zero-medication interventions for managing glucose curves, improving insulin sensitivity, and optimising nutrient partitioning. All are incorporated into your programme.

🥗
Eat protein and vegetables first
Starting meals with protein and fibre before eating carbohydrates reduces peak glucose by 30–40%. The mechanism: protein triggers GLP-1 release (slowing gastric emptying), fibre physically slows glucose absorption, and fat reduces the rate of carbohydrate digestion. Takes zero additional time or cost.
🚶
Walk for 10–15 minutes after meals
Post-meal walking lowers peak glucose by 20–30% by contracting muscle and activating GLUT4 glucose transport independent of insulin. Even slow walking (3–4 km/h) is sufficient. This is the single easiest glucose management intervention available and has outsized impact relative to effort.
🍎
Prioritise fibre: 30g per day minimum
Soluble fibre (oats, legumes, apples, psyllium) slows glucose absorption and feeds gut microbiome that directly produces insulin-sensitising short-chain fatty acids. Insoluble fibre (vegetables, whole grains) improves bowel transit and reduces carbohydrate exposure time. Most adults consume 12–15g daily — half of target.
🏋️
Resistance training: the most powerful insulin sensitiser
Resistance training improves insulin sensitivity for 24–48 hours after each session by upregulating GLUT4 expression in muscle cells. Larger muscle mass means a larger permanent glucose sink. A single session of moderate resistance training improves next-day glucose tolerance by 20–35%. This is why training consistency matters beyond aesthetics.
😴
Sleep: the insulin sensitivity reset
A single night of poor sleep (under 6 hours) reduces insulin sensitivity by 20–25% the following day — equivalent to gaining 4–5kg of body fat in terms of metabolic impact. Sleep deprivation also elevates cortisol, which raises fasting glucose and compounds the problem. Protecting sleep is non-negotiable for glucose management.
⏱️
Time-restricted eating: extending the fasted window
Compressing eating to an 8–12 hour window naturally limits total insulin exposure time. The fasted window (12–16 hours) allows complete insulin clearance, glucagon rise, and sustained fat oxidation. This is not caloric restriction — studies show metabolic benefits independent of calories. The mechanism is time in the fasted state, not deprivation.
🥤
Vinegar before carbohydrate-heavy meals
1–2 tablespoons of apple cider vinegar in water before a high-carbohydrate meal reduces peak glucose by 20–30%. The acetic acid slows gastric emptying and reduces amylase activity (the enzyme that breaks starch into glucose). The evidence is solid. The mechanism is clear. It is not a biohack — it is basic food chemistry.
How your programme manages insulin

Every timing decision in your daily plan reflects insulin logic. None of it is arbitrary.

01
No caffeine for 90 minutes post-wake
Cortisol peaks 30–45 minutes after waking. Cortisol raises blood glucose, which triggers insulin. Adding caffeine in this window elevates both cortisol and glucose further — producing a larger insulin pulse before you've eaten anything. Waiting 90 minutes allows cortisol to begin clearing before caffeine is added. This preserves the fasted state longer and reduces the first insulin spike of the day.
02
Eating window: 12:00–20:00 (or your personalised window)
A 12–16 hour overnight fast keeps insulin suppressed while you sleep and for several hours after waking. This window is where the programme achieves its fat-oxidation time. Breaking the fast with protein rather than carbohydrates produces a smaller initial insulin pulse and establishes satiety hormones for the remainder of the eating window.
03
Pre-training: high carbs, low fat, 90 minutes before
Carbohydrates before training serve two purposes: glycogen availability and the timing of the insulin pulse. Eating 90 minutes before training means insulin has peaked and begun to clear by the time you start training. Working muscle is highly insulin sensitive — the residual insulin directs glucose specifically into muscle cells rather than fat cells.
04
Post-training: fast protein + fast carbs within 30 minutes
The 30-minute post-training window is when muscle insulin sensitivity is at its absolute peak. mTOR is activated. GLUT4 is at the cell surface. A deliberate insulin pulse here — fast carbs + leucine-rich protein — drives nutrients directly into the muscle you just trained. This is the one time in the day where a rapid glucose spike is the desired outcome, not a problem to be managed.
05
Evening: close the eating window by 20:00
Insulin sensitivity follows a circadian rhythm — it declines in the evening as melatonin rises. The same meal eaten at 8pm produces a larger glucose spike and higher insulin response than the same meal at 12pm. Closing the eating window by 8pm allows insulin to clear completely before sleep, which preserves deep sleep architecture (GH is suppressed by elevated insulin) and maximises overnight fat oxidation.
The full picture
Your programme is not a meal plan — it is an insulin management protocol. The fast creates the fat-burning window. The training session creates the anabolic window. The post-training nutrition uses that window. The evening close protects sleep quality and overnight fat oxidation. Every timing decision connects back to insulin.
