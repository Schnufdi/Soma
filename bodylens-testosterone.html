This page covers the lifestyle science of testosterone optimisation. It is not medical advice. Testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) is a clinical decision that requires blood testing and medical supervision. If you suspect clinically low testosterone (symptoms + blood results), see your GP for a full hormonal panel.
The testosterone system — what matters and what doesn't
Most content on testosterone optimisation is either overstated (supplements that barely move the needle) or undersells what lifestyle genuinely achieves. The evidence for lifestyle intervention on testosterone levels is strong, specific, and sufficient to make a measurable difference to the men who implement it consistently.
1
Established
What testosterone actually does — the full picture
Testosterone's role extends well beyond libido and muscle. It is the primary anabolic signal for muscle protein synthesis, the primary regulator of red blood cell production, a key modulator of mood and motivation (via dopamine pathway sensitisation), a driver of fat distribution (visceral vs subcutaneous), and a determinant of bone density. The symptoms of declining testosterone in men 35+ — reduced strength gains despite training, increasing belly fat without dietary change, reduced motivation and assertiveness, longer recovery from training, declining libido — are often attributed to "getting older" when they are attributable to a specific, measurable, partially addressable hormonal change. Knowing whether testosterone is actually declining (via blood testing) separates men who genuinely need clinical intervention from those who need lifestyle optimisation.
2
Established
The HPTA axis — how testosterone production is regulated
The hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis (HPTA) regulates testosterone via a feedback loop. The hypothalamus releases GnRH → pituitary releases LH and FSH → testes produce testosterone → rising testosterone signals the hypothalamus to reduce GnRH. The system maintains set-point levels. Lifestyle factors do not override the set point — they determine where within the range the set point sits. Chronic stress, sleep deprivation, high body fat, and low micronutrient availability all lower the set point. Resistance training, adequate sleep, and optimal body composition raise it. This is why lifestyle intervention cannot substitute for clinical TRT when testosterone is genuinely deficient — but it absolutely determines whether you are at the top or bottom of your natural range.
3
Established
The cortisol-testosterone antagonism
Cortisol and testosterone share a precursor (pregnenolone) and compete for steroidogenic resources. Chronically elevated cortisol — from overtraining, under-recovery, psychological stress, or sleep deprivation — directly suppresses testosterone production at both the hypothalamic and testicular level. This is why the "work harder, train more" approach to testosterone is counterproductive when recovery is inadequate. The men with the highest natural testosterone levels are not the ones training the most volume — they are the ones training with adequate intensity and recovering adequately. Recovery is not the passive part of training. It is where testosterone is produced.

Sleep — the primary testosterone lever
The majority of testosterone production occurs during sleep, concentrated in the slow-wave and early REM cycles. Sleep deprivation is not a minor inconvenience for testosterone — it is the most potent testosterone suppressant in the common lifestyle arsenal, producing measurable declines within days.
1
Established
5 days of restricted sleep drops testosterone 10-15% in young men
A landmark University of Chicago study restricted young healthy men to 5 hours of sleep for one week. Daytime testosterone levels fell by 10-15% — equivalent to the testosterone decline of 10-15 years of normal ageing. The effect was rapid, significant, and reversed on sleep restoration. This is not a subtle effect. One week of inadequate sleep produces the hormonal equivalent of a decade of ageing. For men who are already at the lower end of their natural range due to age, chronic sleep restriction is particularly costly — it removes the hormonal buffer that good sleep provides against the natural age-related decline.
2
Established
The nocturnal testosterone pulse — why sleep timing matters
Testosterone secretion follows a clear diurnal pattern: it rises during early sleep, peaks in the early morning (approximately 6-8 AM in most men), and falls throughout the day to its nadir in the evening. The morning testosterone peak is driven by the LH pulses that occur during slow-wave sleep in the first half of the night. Alcohol — which suppresses slow-wave sleep — blunts these pulses. Inconsistent sleep timing disrupts the LH pulse pattern. Late-night training that delays sleep onset shortens the early-night SWS window. All three suppress the morning testosterone peak independently of total sleep time.
3
Established
Sleep quality vs quantity — both matter
7 hours of fragmented, poor-quality sleep does not produce the same testosterone as 7 hours of consolidated, adequate SWS sleep. Alcohol at dinner — even 2-3 units — fragments sleep and reduces testosterone despite nominal sleep duration. Sleep apnoea (very common in men 35+ and substantially underdiagnosed) produces significant testosterone suppression through chronic sleep fragmentation. If testosterone symptoms exist with apparently adequate sleep duration, sleep apnoea and alcohol consumption are the two highest-yield investigations before assuming clinical hypogonadism.

Training — the most controllable testosterone lever
Resistance training produces acute testosterone elevations and, over months, upregulates the HPTA set point. But training volume, intensity, and recovery balance all determine whether training is a testosterone stimulus or a testosterone suppressant.
1
Established
Compound multi-joint exercises produce the greatest acute T response
The acute testosterone response to training is proportional to the muscle mass recruited and the intensity of effort. Compound movements — squats, deadlifts, bench press, rows, overhead press — produce significantly greater acute testosterone elevation than isolation exercises. The mechanism: greater metabolic demand, higher lactate production, and greater motor unit recruitment all amplify the hormonal response. High-intensity (RPE 7-9) multi-joint training produces an acute testosterone elevation of 15-25% above baseline, peaking 15-30 minutes post-exercise. This acute response, repeated consistently over months, contributes to the chronic upregulation of testosterone production capacity.
2
Established
Overtraining and the testosterone-cortisol ratio
The testosterone-to-cortisol (T/C) ratio is a validated marker of anabolic/catabolic training state. When training volume or frequency exceeds recovery capacity, cortisol chronically elevates and testosterone chronically suppresses — the T/C ratio falls, indicating a net catabolic state. Men who train 6-7 days per week without adequate recovery often have worse testosterone profiles than men training 3-4 days with adequate sleep and nutrition. The signal for overtraining is the same as the signal for clinical low testosterone: declining strength, persistent fatigue, reduced motivation, body composition stagnation. Blood testing distinguishes them — but the initial management is identical: reduce volume, improve sleep, eat more.
3
Emerging evidence
Zone 2 cardio and testosterone — a nuanced relationship
Endurance training has a complicated relationship with testosterone. High-volume endurance training (marathon/triathlon training, 10+ hours/week) is associated with lower testosterone in men — likely via chronic cortisol elevation and energy availability reduction. However, moderate Zone 2 cardio (2-4 hours/week) does not suppress testosterone and improves the metabolic conditions that support it: better insulin sensitivity, lower visceral fat, improved sleep quality. The prescription: resistance training as the primary testosterone driver, Zone 2 as metabolic support. Avoid allowing cardio volume to exceed recovery capacity.

Body fat & oestrogen — the conversion pathway
Adipose (fat) tissue contains the enzyme aromatase, which converts testosterone to oestradiol (a form of oestrogen). This means high body fat — particularly visceral fat — directly reduces free testosterone and raises oestrogen in men. Managing body fat is one of the most direct testosterone interventions available.
1
Established
Aromatase and the testosterone-oestrogen conversion
Aromatase (the CYP19A1 enzyme) converts androgens to oestrogens. Every man produces oestrogen — it is essential for libido, bone density, mood, and cardiovascular health in men. The problem arises when the conversion rate is too high. Visceral fat expresses significantly more aromatase than subcutaneous fat — meaning abdominal fat accumulation creates a self-reinforcing cycle: more visceral fat → more aromatase → lower testosterone → reduced muscle maintenance → more fat accumulation. The cycle is breakable but requires addressing body fat directly, not just supplementing testosterone.
2
Established
Oestrogen in men — what the right level looks like
Men need oestradiol in the range of 90–180 pmol/L. Below this range (which can occur in men aggressively suppressing oestrogen), bone density falls, libido decreases, and cardiovascular risk rises. Above this range (which occurs with high body fat and high aromatase activity), gynecomastia, reduced libido, emotional dysregulation, and impaired testosterone production occur. The goal is not to suppress oestrogen but to optimise the testosterone-to-oestrogen ratio by raising testosterone and reducing aromatase activity through lower body fat. Men who use aromatase inhibitors without addressing body fat are treating a symptom of a system problem.
3
Established
Sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG) — why total T is an incomplete picture
SHBG binds testosterone in the bloodstream, rendering it biologically inactive. Only "free" testosterone (unbound) can exert androgenic effects. A man can have a total testosterone at the top of the reference range but have clinically low free testosterone due to high SHBG. Conversely, a man with moderate total testosterone and low SHBG has high free testosterone. SHBG rises with age, excessive caloric restriction, liver stress (alcohol), and certain medications. For a complete picture, a testosterone panel should include total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, and LH — not just total T. Your GP can arrange this if symptoms warrant investigation.

Nutrition & micronutrients — what the evidence actually supports
The supplement market for testosterone is dominated by products with weak evidence. But specific micronutrients have strong, mechanistic evidence for supporting testosterone production — primarily because deficiency in these nutrients directly impairs the enzymatic pathways that produce testosterone.
1
Established
Zinc — the most important testosterone micronutrient
Zinc is an essential cofactor for 5-alpha reductase (the enzyme that converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone) and for LH signalling at the testicular level. Zinc deficiency — common in athletes due to sweat losses, and in men eating low red meat diets — directly suppresses testosterone production. Supplementation in genuinely zinc-deficient men reliably raises testosterone; supplementation in non-deficient men does not. Dietary sources: red meat, shellfish (particularly oysters), pumpkin seeds, eggs. If supplementing: zinc bisglycinate or picolinate (15-30mg elemental zinc), taken with food, balanced with copper supplementation to avoid displacement. Testing serum zinc before supplementing is preferable to blind supplementation.
2
Established
Vitamin D — a steroid hormone precursor
Vitamin D is technically a steroid hormone — not a vitamin — with receptors in testicular Leydig cells (the primary site of testosterone production). Testosterone production requires adequate vitamin D signalling. Multiple studies show a positive correlation between vitamin D levels and total testosterone; one RCT showed a significant testosterone increase in deficient men following 12 months of D3 supplementation (3332 IU/day). The UK's chronic vitamin D deficiency (>50% of adults are deficient, particularly in winter) makes this a realistic testosterone lever for a significant proportion of men. Serum 25(OH)D target: 75-125 nmol/L. Standard NHS supplement dose (400 IU) is insufficient to reach optimal levels — 2000-4000 IU daily is typically required.
3
Established
Dietary fat — the testosterone substrate
Testosterone is synthesised from cholesterol. Diets that severely restrict dietary fat — particularly saturated fat — reduce testosterone production by limiting substrate availability. Studies comparing low-fat (<20% kcal from fat) to moderate-fat (40%+ kcal from fat) diets consistently show 10-15% lower testosterone on the low-fat diet. This does not mean very high fat intake raises testosterone linearly — the relationship plateaus. It means caloric deficits that eliminate dietary fat (very low fat + calorie restriction) add hormonal suppression to the metabolic stress of restriction. Adequate dietary fat (25-35% of total calories, including some saturated fat from whole food sources) is a testosterone prerequisite, not a luxury.
4
Emerging evidence
Ashwagandha (KSM-66 or Sensoril) — the best-evidenced testosterone supplement
Several double-blind RCTs show KSM-66 ashwagandha (300-600mg/day) increasing total testosterone by 10-17% and improving cortisol by 22-30% over 8-12 weeks. The mechanism is via cortisol suppression — lower cortisol reduces HPA axis suppression of testosterone. This is not a minor effect and the evidence quality is better than almost any other testosterone supplement. However, the effect is most pronounced in men with elevated stress and cortisol — it is not a direct testosterone stimulant, it removes a cortisol-mediated ceiling. Men with normal cortisol levels will see smaller effects.

Testing & TRT — when to investigate and what to measure
Testosterone testing is straightforward and increasingly accessible. Knowing your numbers removes the guesswork from lifestyle optimisation and establishes whether clinical intervention is appropriate — or whether the lifestyle levers have not yet been properly applied.
1
Established
What to test and when
Blood should be drawn in the morning (7-10 AM) when testosterone peaks. A single measurement is insufficient — testosterone varies 15-20% day to day. Two measurements on separate days are the clinical standard for diagnosis. The minimum useful panel: total testosterone, free testosterone (or SHBG to calculate it), LH, SHBG, oestradiol, and haematocrit. Context markers worth including if accessible: sex hormone binding globulin alone does not tell you direction — include LH to determine whether low T is primary (testicular) or secondary (hypothalamic/pituitary). If LH is low alongside low testosterone, the cause is upstream and requires different management than primary testicular failure.

TRT — when it is and isn't appropriate
TRT is clinically appropriate for men with confirmed hypogonadism — two morning bloods below 10 nmol/L (or free T below normal range) with symptoms. It is not appropriate as a lifestyle optimisation tool for men within the normal range who feel their testosterone "could be higher." TRT in men with normal testosterone suppresses endogenous production permanently (via HPTA feedback suppression) — it is a long-term commitment, not a short-term experiment. For men in the 10-15 nmol/L range with clear symptoms, the evidence-based sequence is: optimise sleep for 12 weeks, reduce body fat if above 20%, address stress and cortisol, test micronutrients (zinc, vitamin D), then retest — before discussing TRT with a urologist or endocrinologist.
