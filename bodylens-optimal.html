Gut & digestion — your second brain
The gut is not a passive tube. It contains 500 million neurons (the enteric nervous system), produces 95% of your body's serotonin, and communicates constantly with the brain via the vagus nerve. Gut health is not a wellness concept — it is a performance variable.
1
The gut-brain axis — a bidirectional highway
The vagus nerve carries signals in both directions between the gut and the brain. 80-90% of vagal fibres carry signals from gut to brain — not the other way around. The gut sends more information to the brain than the brain sends to the gut. What happens in the gut directly affects mood, anxiety, cognitive function, and stress response. Gut inflammation produces neuroinflammation. Poor microbiome diversity correlates with depression, anxiety, and impaired cognitive function — through multiple independent pathways.
2
The Bristol Stool Chart — your daily diagnostic
The most accessible real-time gut health marker available. What you produce tells you whether your gut transit time is optimal, whether fibre and hydration are adequate, and whether inflammation is present. Types 3-4 are target. Types 1-2 indicate insufficient fibre/water or impaired motility. Types 5-7 indicate too-fast transit — infection, inflammation, food sensitivity, or stress.

The microbiome — diversity is the performance variable
The gut microbiome contains approximately 38 trillion microorganisms — roughly equal to the number of human cells in your body. Microbiome diversity (the number of different species) is the primary marker of gut health. Low diversity correlates with: depression, anxiety, obesity, type 2 diabetes, inflammatory bowel disease, and impaired immune function. The interventions with the strongest evidence for improving diversity: dietary fibre (diverse sources), fermented foods (kefir, kimchi, yoghurt), polyphenols (berries, olive oil, green tea), and avoiding unnecessary antibiotics.

Energy systems — the four-lever model
Chronic low energy is almost never a single-cause problem. It is usually a combination of glycaemic instability, poor sleep architecture, mitochondrial function, and adrenal/cortisol patterns. Addressing all four simultaneously produces dramatically better results than any single intervention.
1
Glycaemic stability — the fastest energy fix
Blood glucose variability is the most common cause of daytime energy crashes. The mechanism: high-GI carbohydrates produce rapid glucose elevation → large insulin response → reactive hypoglycaemia → energy crash, hunger, brain fog. The fix: protein and fibre before or with carbohydrates at every meal slows glucose absorption. Walking 10 minutes after meals reduces postprandial glucose spike by 20-30%. The energy patterns that feel like "metabolism" are often just glycaemic stability.
2
Mitochondrial function — the deeper energy lever
Mitochondria produce ATP — the energy currency of every cell. Mitochondrial dysfunction produces fatigue, cognitive impairment, and reduced exercise capacity that is not explained by poor sleep or diet alone. Key interventions: Zone 2 exercise (the primary stimulus for mitochondrial biogenesis), CoQ10 (a direct mitochondrial cofactor, particularly relevant after 40 and with statin use), adequate B vitamins and iron (essential cofactors), and avoiding excessive alcohol (directly damages mitochondrial membranes).
3
Cortisol and the afternoon crash — the adrenal connection
The cortisol rhythm should peak within 30-60 minutes of waking (the cortisol awakening response) and decline throughout the day, reaching its lowest point around sleep onset. Afternoon energy crashes often reflect a flattened cortisol curve — either too low overall (from chronic stress and poor sleep exhausting the cortisol response) or too high in the afternoon (from coffee consumed at the wrong time). Ashwagandha (KSM-66 or Sensoril form) has the strongest evidence base for normalising the cortisol curve.

Brain & focus — the cognitive performance stack
Cognitive performance — focus, working memory, processing speed, creative thinking — is trainable and supplementable. The ceiling is much higher than most people experience day-to-day.
1
Sleep is the primary cognitive performance lever — more than any supplement
Cognitive performance degrades in a dose-dependent, linear relationship with sleep deprivation — but critically, sleep-deprived people are poor judges of their own impairment. After 5-6 days of sub-7h sleep, cognitive performance is equivalent to a full night of total sleep deprivation — yet subjective alertness has partially adapted, creating a false confidence. Most people chronically underperform cognitively due to accumulated sleep debt they have normalised.
2
The caffeine + L-Theanine combination — the best-evidenced cognitive stack
Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, producing alertness. L-Theanine promotes alpha-wave activity, reducing the anxious edge of caffeine while enhancing its alertness effect. The combination produces better focused attention than either alone — with fewer side effects. Optimal ratio: 100mg caffeine : 200mg L-Theanine. Time caffeine 90 minutes post-wake (after the cortisol awakening response clears) to avoid cortisol-adenosine competition that reduces caffeine's effectiveness.
3
Exercise and BDNF — the most powerful cognitive enhancement available
Aerobic exercise produces Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) — sometimes called "Miracle-Gro for the brain." BDNF drives neuroplasticity, supports memory consolidation, and is protective against cognitive decline. A single bout of moderate aerobic exercise (20-30 minutes, 65-75% max HR) produces a BDNF spike that enhances learning and memory formation for approximately 2-3 hours. Training before cognitively demanding work is neurologically superior to training after it.

Anxiety & stress — the hormonal roots
Anxiety is a physiological state, not just a mental one. It has measurable hormonal, neurological, and biochemical correlates — and it has physiological interventions that are as effective as psychological ones for many people.
1
The HPA axis — the stress-response system
The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis is the primary stress response system. Perceived threat → hypothalamus releases CRH → pituitary releases ACTH → adrenal glands release cortisol. The system is designed for acute, short-term threats — not chronic, sustained psychological stress. Chronic HPA activation produces: elevated baseline cortisol, impaired immune function, reduced testosterone, disrupted sleep architecture, and hippocampal atrophy (reduced memory and emotional regulation capacity).
2
Physiological sigh — the fastest acute anxiety reducer
The physiological sigh is a naturally occurring breathing pattern — a double inhale through the nose followed by a long exhale through the mouth — that reinflates collapsed alveoli and rapidly shifts the nervous system from sympathetic (stress) to parasympathetic (calm) dominance. The exhale-dominant ratio is the mechanism: long exhale → decreased thoracic pressure → less afferent signalling from the heart → reduced perceived arousal. One to three physiological sighs produce measurable anxiety reduction within 30-60 seconds.
3
Magnesium — the underappreciated anxiolytic
Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions including GABA synthesis (the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter — the molecule that "calms" neural activity). Magnesium deficiency — which is widespread, particularly in athletes — reduces GABA tone and directly increases anxiety and stress sensitivity. Magnesium glycinate (high bioavailability, crosses blood-brain barrier) at 300-400mg before sleep: reduces anxiety symptoms, improves sleep onset, and reduces cortisol awakening response.

Mood & happiness — the biology of feeling good
Mood is substantially biological. The neurotransmitters, hormones, and gut-brain signals that determine baseline mood are all modifiable — through training, sleep, nutrition, social connection, and targeted supplementation.
1
Dopamine — the motivation and reward molecule
Dopamine drives motivation, goal-directed behaviour, and the anticipatory pleasure of pursuit. Low dopamine baseline produces: low motivation, reduced ability to experience pleasure (anhedonia), poor focus, and increased addictive behaviour. Dopamine is not released by achieving goals — it is released in anticipation of goals. The intervention: set challenging, achievable short-term goals that create regular dopamine cycles. Avoid excessive dopamine "hits" from low-effort sources (social media, junk food) — they raise the threshold required for dopamine response and blunt baseline motivation.
2
Serotonin — the contentment molecule, built in the gut
95% of serotonin is produced in the gut — by enterochromaffin cells that respond to gut microbiome composition, dietary tryptophan availability, and the mechanical stimulation of gut motility. Gut microbiome disruption directly reduces serotonin production. The evidence for gut-brain axis interventions on mood: probiotic supplementation has shown measurable reductions in depression and anxiety scores in multiple RCTs — the effect size is modest but real. Dietary tryptophan (turkey, eggs, dairy, seeds) is the serotonin precursor; carbohydrates at the same meal improve tryptophan transport across the blood-brain barrier.
3
Training is a more powerful antidepressant than most people realise
Exercise produces: BDNF (neuroprotective, antidepressant), endorphins and endocannabinoids (acute mood elevation), reduced cortisol over time, improved sleep, and increased social interaction. A 2023 Lancet Psychiatry meta-analysis of 1,039 trials found exercise more effective than SSRIs for depression — with particular benefit from strength training and high-intensity exercise. The effect is dose-dependent and begins within the first session.

Hormones — the chemical messengers that run everything
Hormone levels are not fixed. They respond to training, sleep, nutrition, stress, and supplementation. Understanding which levers move which hormones — and in which direction — is the difference between managing your body and being managed by it.
1
Testosterone — the anabolic, libido, and mood hormone
Testosterone declines at 1-2% per year from approximately age 30 in men. In women, testosterone is present at lower levels but is equally important for libido, mood, and muscle maintenance. The most powerful natural testosterone support interventions: resistance training (particularly compound movements at high intensity), adequate sleep (GH and testosterone both peak during deep sleep), zinc and magnesium sufficiency, body fat 10-20% range (below and above this range testosterone declines), and stress management (cortisol directly antagonises testosterone production).
2
Oestrogen — critical for women, present in men
Oestrogen drives bone density, cardiovascular protection, mood stability, insulin sensitivity, and cognitive function in women. Its decline in perimenopause (typically beginning mid-40s, sometimes earlier) produces: mood instability, sleep disruption, reduced bone density, hot flushes, and changes in body composition and fat distribution. Resistance training is the strongest lifestyle intervention for managing the oestrogen transition — it maintains bone density, improves insulin sensitivity, reduces visceral fat, and supports mood through independent pathways. For men, oestrogen (from aromatisation of testosterone) is essential for bone density and cardiovascular health — but excess aromatisation (from high body fat and alcohol) is counterproductive.
3
Growth hormone — the repair and recovery signal
GH peaks during the first slow-wave sleep cycle (typically 60-90 minutes after sleep onset) and after resistance training. GH drives: muscle protein synthesis, fat oxidation, cellular repair, and collagen production. The primary interventions for maintaining GH output: protect slow-wave sleep (the GH pulse is abolished by alcohol, sleep fragmentation, and late eating), resistance training at high intensity (training to RPE 8-9 produces the largest acute GH response), and intermittent fasting or an extended overnight fast (insulin suppression during the fast allows GH to rise).
