What needs fixing first
No persistent backend
Everything lives in localStorage. Switch device, clear browser, use incognito — profile gone. You cannot build a subscription business on this. Priority one.
No progress arc
A day-1 user and a month-6 user have the same experience. The product doesn't visibly compound. No trend graphs, no progression evidence, no "look how far you've come."
Onboarding drop-off
33 questions is the right depth for personalisation, wrong experience for conversion. Most people won't complete it. Needs restructuring to capture 10 critical variables first, deepen over time.
Deployment fragility
Manual GitHub uploads, nav drift, file corruption risk. Acceptable for building fast. Unacceptable at 5,000 users. Needs a proper CI/CD pipeline before scale.
Content engine not deployed
The best acquisition channel — organic search via science content — hasn't been switched on. Every week this isn't running is compounding interest left on the table.

Retention engineering

What makes people stay.

The economics of subscription only work if people stay. At 8% monthly churn you need to acquire 8% just to stand still. These are the specific features that compound retention — not the flashy ones, the ones that make the product feel like it genuinely knows you.

🧠
Progressive personalisation
The plan in month three must be visibly better than month one — because it actually knows you better. Sleep patterns, training response, food preferences, deviation tendencies. Users feel this. When something knows you that well, you don't leave it.
Drives months 2-6 retention
📆
Weekly coach review
Every Sunday, a short AI-generated debrief. Not a stats dump — a narrative. "You hit protein five of seven days. The two you missed were both late evenings. That's a pattern, not a failure." A weekly touchpoint that creates the habit of opening the app even when nothing specific is pulling them in.
Most powerful single retention feature
📊
Visible progress arc
Progress made invisible is progress that doesn't retain. Body composition trends, training progression, protein consistency, weight trajectory. Not just numbers — the coach's interpretation of what those numbers mean for the goal they told you about on day one.
Critical for months 3-12
🔬
Body scan loop
Every time someone scans and their numbers change, targets recalculate and the plan shifts. A concrete demonstration that the product is responsive to reality, not static. Creates a reason to scan regularly — which creates a habit loop — which creates retention.
Drives quarterly re-engagement
🤝
Coach memory
Every conversation, every SOS moment, every deviation logged accumulates into a picture the coach explicitly references. "You mentioned last week that evening meals were hardest — this week's plan adjusted for that." That's not a feature. That's a relationship. Relationships are sticky.
Locks in after month 2
⚡
SOS emergency fixes
The moment you rescue someone from an off-plan spiral — starving, skipped gym, terrible sleep, ate the whole pizza — and they get a specific, personal, useful answer in 30 seconds, they tell someone. Crisis handling is a retention and acquisition mechanism simultaneously.
Word-of-mouth trigger
Competitive advantage

The moat.

In a saturated market, winning is about being categorically different — not marginally better. These are the three defensible advantages that get harder to replicate the longer the product runs.

01 — Primary moat
Science architecture
The reasoning layer — the actual explanations of why things work, grounded in real mechanisms — is not a feature, it's a category. Competitors can copy the interface in a week. They cannot copy 18 years of institutional knowledge about how performance actually works in the real world, at the level of a person who has lived it professionally.

This is the moat. Everything else is built on it.
02 — Data moat
Accumulated personalisation
At 12 months of daily use, BodyLens knows more about how your body specifically responds to training, sleep, and nutrition than any other system. That knowledge is locked to the platform. Switching means starting from scratch.

This is the retention moat. It compounds invisibly for the first six months and becomes nearly unassailable by month twelve.
03 — Trust moat
Credibility at scale
Health products live or die on trust. Being wrong in health has real consequences. The brand that builds a reputation for getting it right — consistently, verifiably, in high-stakes moments — becomes very hard to displace.

The coach voice, the science citations, the honest acknowledgment of what we don't know — these compound into institutional credibility that can't be manufactured quickly.
Risk register

Three things that kill it.

These are not speculative risks. They are the specific patterns that have killed every otherwise-good subscription health product. Know them. Build against them deliberately.

Building features instead of fixing retention
Every hour spent on a new feature while monthly churn is above 8% is misdirected. The product is already good enough to retain people — if you understand why they leave. Find out why they leave before building anything new.

The data exists in cancellation surveys, in the sessions people don't open, in the onboarding questions they skip. Read it obsessively.
Trying to be everywhere on social
You don't have the bandwidth for five content channels and they're the wrong channels for this product. Fitness on social means before-and-after photos, transformation content, motivational copy. That's not BodyLens.

One focused channel — long-form science content that earns organic search placement — done exceptionally well, beats five channels done adequately every time.
Waiting until it's perfect to charge
Start charging now. Not because you need the money but because paying users give you honest feedback in a way free users never do. The person who paid £14.99 and is thinking about cancelling will tell you exactly what's wrong if you ask them directly.

That information is worth more than any amount of user research, product analytics, or founder intuition.
